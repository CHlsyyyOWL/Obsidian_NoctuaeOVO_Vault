The key idea behind the **credit channel** is that monetary policy affects firms **differently** depending on their ability to access credit. The reasoning follows:

1. **Smaller firms and firms with less collateralizable assets** rely more on bank loans because they have limited internal funds and cannot easily issue bonds or stocks to raise capital.

2. When monetary policy tightens (higher interest rates, reduced money supply), banks cut back on lending. These credit-dependent firms face greater borrowing constraints, reducing their investment and output more than firms with easier access to credit.

3. Conversely, when monetary policy loosens (lower interest rates, increased money supply), banks are more willing to lend, and these credit-constrained firms experience a **larger** boost in investment and output compared to firms that can already access external financing.

This **asymmetric effect** aligns with the **credit channel hypothesis**, which emphasizes that monetary policy affects the economy not just by changing interest rates (as in the standard interest rate channel), but also by influencing credit availability—especially for firms that depend on it the most.

If firms that **can** collateralize assets (i.e., have strong balance sheets) were the ones most affected by monetary policy, *it would suggest that financing constraints play a smaller role, weakening the argument for the credit channel.
