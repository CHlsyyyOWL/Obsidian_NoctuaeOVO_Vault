> ### **Introduction: The Economic Depression of the 1930s**


Hook：How do ideological narratives reframe economic insecurity as political threat, thereby legitimising nationalism and exclusionary boundary-making in the 1930s?

The global economic depression of the early 1930s constituted a profound structural shock rather than a temporary downturn. Following the 1929 Wall Street Crash, international trade collapsed, unemployment rose sharply, and state revenues contracted across both industrial and colonial economies. In Europe and North America, mass unemployment and industrial stagnation undermined confidence in liberal capitalism.

Dogliani describes the depression as a _“world economic crisis characterised by unemployment, fiscal strain, and industrial malaise”_, which fundamentally altered the relationship between the state and society (Dogliani). Even where governments attempted to deny the existence of crisis—as in Fascist Italy—the material consequences were felt in declining consumption, social instability, and fears of disorder.

In colonial contexts, the effects were uneven. As Kong shows for Hong Kong, official reports suggested that the colony was _less severely affected_ than neighbouring regions. Yet the global depression still formed the backdrop against which social anxiety, political debate, and identity conflict intensified (Kong).

Thus, the depression should be understood not only as an economic event, but as a catalyst for political and ideological transformation. But how? 

---

### Background: From Economic Crisis to Political Threat

Economic hardship did not remain confined to material concerns. Across different societies, it was rapidly reframed as a political and social threat. People worried not only about jobs and wages, but about order, status, and national survival.

In Hong Kong, white British residents feared the erosion of their _prestige_ and _respectability_ within the colonial racial hierarchy. Even without mass unemployment, economic uncertainty was interpreted as a threat to white dominance and imperial authority. Anxiety focused on whether Europeans would lose privileged access to jobs and social standing (Kong).

In the United States, the Depression generated fears of social breakdown, radicalism, and disorder. The New Deal’s public works programmes were explicitly justified as preventing idleness, unrest, and political extremism among unemployed men.

In Italy, the Fascist regime viewed unemployment and economic insecurity as dangers to social discipline and regime legitimacy. Dogliani shows that anxiety centred on the figure of the _unemployed, idle male_, imagined as both economically unproductive and politically destabilising.
Across these cases, economic insecurity was reinterpreted as a threat to social order, political authority, and collective identity.

These political anxieties did not exist in a vacuum. They were channelled and stabilised through **ideological movements**, particularly **communism, fascism, and imperialism**, which together reshaped nationalism in the 1930s.
Communism framed economic crisis as evidence of capitalist failure and redefined political loyalty around class struggle and socialist patriotism. As Mevius argues, communist regimes did not reject nationalism; instead, they incorporated it through concepts such as _socialist patriotism_, presenting the state as the true representative of the nation.
Fascism responded by rejecting both liberalism and international socialism. It transformed nationalism into an **all-encompassing ideology**, emphasising unity, discipline, masculinity, and sacrifice. Economic problems were not debated openly but absorbed into narratives of national regeneration.
Imperialism, particularly in colonial settings, reframed crisis as a challenge to racial and imperial hierarchy. Economic uncertainty intensified fears about the loss of imperial authority, producing exclusionary and racialised forms of nationalism.
In this sense, nationalism in the 1930s was not a single ideology, but a shared political language through which competing systems responded to crisis.

### **Part I – What Did Governments Do?**

### British Legacy in Hong Kong: 
The Hong Kong case is analysed by **[Author: Kong]**, a historian of empire and colonial identity. Writing within the framework of _global 1930s history_, Kong situates Hong Kong within wider debates about whiteness, imperial anxiety, and economic crisis.
The central argument of the book/article is that **the Great Depression intensified racial and status anxieties among white British residents**, even though Hong Kong did not experience severe economic collapse. Rather than material deprivation, what mattered most was the fear of losing **imperial prestige and racial authority**.
Kong challenges the assumption that economic hardship automatically produces class-based politics. Instead, the Hong Kong case demonstrates how **economic uncertainty can reinforce racial and imperial forms of nationalism**.

---
### **British Legacy in Hong Kong: Background, Timeline, Events**

Following the onset of the global depression, the Hong Kong government established an **Economic Commission in 1934** to assess its impact. The commission concluded that unemployment was limited, public order remained stable, and overall living standards had not dramatically declined.

Despite this, public debate among white residents increasingly focused on the protection of “British” employment and status. The **White British League** emerged as a platform advocating preferential treatment for white Britons and restrictions on other Europeans and local populations.
This period saw intensified discussion of _who counted as British_, who deserved imperial protection, and how racial hierarchy should be preserved under conditions of uncertainty.
Kong emphasises that these debates were not marginal, but reflected deeper anxieties about imperial decline and global instability.

---

### Analysis: Depression, Tension, Nationalism and Imperialism in Hong Kong**

The Hong Kong case demonstrates that **economic depression does not need to produce mass poverty to generate political radicalisation**. Instead, depression heightened awareness of vulnerability within the imperial order.

Economic uncertainty became a **status anxiety**: white residents feared slipping down the colonial hierarchy. This anxiety was translated into **exclusionary nationalism**, defined not by shared sacrifice but by racial boundaries.

Imperialism shaped this response. Rather than mobilising the entire population, colonial nationalism worked by **defending privilege**, preserving hierarchy, and reinforcing racial distinctions.
Thus, rising tension in Hong Kong reflected not economic collapse, but **fear of imperial decline**, revealing how nationalism can operate defensively rather than expansively.

### **Part II – Fascism in Italy
For the case of Italy, we are using our reading material: Environemnt and leisure in Italy during fascism. It is written by Patrizia Dogliani, whose work focuses on everyday life, environment, and leisure under Fascism. Writing against purely ideological interpretations of Fascism, Dogliani emphasises governance, social policy, and lived experience.
The central argument of his work is that Fascist Italy responded to economic crisis not by acknowledging depression, but by **reframing it through policies of mass mobilisation**, especially in leisure, sport, and environmental projects.
These policies were not peripheral. They were central to Fascism’s attempt to create a disciplined, loyal, and productive national community.

---

### **Fascism in Italy: Parks, Exercise, Public Works**

The Fascist regime expanded **parks, recreational spaces, sports organisations, and mass excursions**. These initiatives aimed to integrate citizens into state-controlled leisure and physical activity.
Youth organisations, workers’ clubs, and organised travel programmes structured free time and promoted ideals of strength, masculinity, and obedience. Public works projects echoed similar programmes elsewhere, such as the American Civilian Conservation Corps, but with explicit ideological goals.
Crucially, these measures targeted **unemployed and underemployed men**, transforming them from potential sources of disorder into symbols of national regeneration.

---

### Why Italy Did This: Depression, Denial, and Fascist Nationalism

Unlike democratic states, Fascist Italy **refused to publicly acknowledge the existence of the Great Depression**. Admitting crisis would undermine the regime’s claim to competence and strength.
Instead, unemployment and insecurity were reframed as issues of **moral discipline and national duty**. Fascism presented itself as the solution to crisis by creating unity, purpose, and direction.
These policies reveal how **rising tension was absorbed into authoritarian nationalism**. Economic anxiety was neutralised not through debate or redistribution, but through mobilisation, spectacle, and control.
Nationalism here was expansive and totalising: the individual existed only as part of the nation.

### **Part III – Analysis & Conclusion

And finally 

> **How do ideologies translate economic anxiety into political anxiety—and ultimately into nationalism?**

Across the three cases, a common mechanism emerges. Economic depression created uncertainty and insecurity. Ideologies then reframed this insecurity as a threat to **order, identity, and authority**. Nationalism offered emotional reassurance, political clarity, and a sense of belonging.
In Hong Kong, nationalism was **imperial and exclusionary**, protecting racial hierarchy. In Italy, nationalism was **authoritarian and mobilising**, integrating society into the state. Communism, as Mevius reminds us, also embraced nationalism by redefining it through ideological loyalty.

The 1930s show that nationalism is not the opposite of ideology, but often its most effective vehicle in times of crisis.
