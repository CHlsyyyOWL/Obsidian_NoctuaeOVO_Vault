## The problem of the British
• The problem for the British is that the trade balance with China is very unfavourable. One solution opium smuggling
•Opium smuggling developed very quickly and with high profit
• Opium was banned: 2 million smokers, trade unbalance
• A Chinese official was appointed to solve the problem, opium deliveries were set fire
• English soldiers took the control of the South of China and the Yangtzi River
• First opium war (1839-1842), the Nanjing Treaty provides Great Britain with very important benefits: 5 ports opened to trade, HK became a British territory, indemnity, etc. extraterritorial rights for foreigners, Trade tarrifs decided by Chinese and English people.
• Second opium war (1856-1860): 10 new ports opened, embassies in Beijing, right to navigate on the Changjiang (Yangtze river), indemnity,

## Consequences of the unequal treaties
The social, political and environmental crisis worsens and the Imperial system collapsed (Abolition of the Empire 1911).
• Decline of craft industry (foreign concurrence), increase of import, more and more peasants without land, increase of criminality (people without jobs) and in number of refugees
• Impoverishment of the population, which led to rebellions and uprisings (for example Taiping rebellion 1851-1864) which, at their turn resulted in an increase in jobless people and refugees.
• Collapse of the state: the state needs more and more money, to cope with rebellions, wars, subsidies, etc. but people have no money to pay taxes. Water conservancy systems are no more maintained, which resulted in numerous
"natural disasters"
• Rebellions: the most important: Taiping 1851-1864 (20/30 million killed).
• Chinese people became second-rank citizens in their own countries.
• A part of the people accept westernisation: christians, merchants, capitalists

## The characteristic of foreign domination: hypo-colonization
- loss of sovereignty but only on portions of the territory 
	- foreigh settlements (shanghai, beijing, wuhan etc)
	- Occupation of outlying territories
- Presence of foreign nationals but very sparsely distributed
	- foreign soldiers
	- Missionaires 
	- Traders and diplomas in big cities
- Foreign companies (commercial then industrial) and sino-foreign companies control the modern economy
- Foreign countries control custom and post office but the rest of the administration remains in the hands of the Chinese government 
- Westernisation (educations, religions, consumption, consumptions) but limited to big cities. Most Chinese never met foreigners
- There are two Chinas
- Only Japan tried to colonise part of China 

# Chinese reactions
Two different positions to foreigners
- To resist foreigners by going back to the golden days. If the foreigners dominate CHina it is because China lost it tradition. To restore the Traditional virtues of China is the only solution. 
- To imitate foreigners, to modernise the country in order to better resist. If the foreigners dominate China it is because China is still a traditional society.

## The failure of first attempt 1895-1911
Economic modernization depends on the action of individuals Li Hongzhang, then limited to an handful of coastal cities (only 600,000 workers around the beginning of the 20th century)
- Lack of capital, Investment in sectors where profits are not very high, many capitalists prefer to collaborate with foreign companies and are not confident in officials
- Local markets limited: foreign concurrence but also low level of living standards
• Ambiguous position of Foreigners.
- They would like to see China developing its economy and then take advantage of domestic demand
- But they do not like concurrence and they prefer to export to China. Foreign countries sill afraid by China (Fu Manchu).
• Shimonoseki treaty: a tuning point. It is not enough to develop economy.
• Consequence: the imperial government gradually lost power to the advantage of local (military) powers
• Most people begin to consider that China needs radical modernization (Westernization) implemented by a strong state.
