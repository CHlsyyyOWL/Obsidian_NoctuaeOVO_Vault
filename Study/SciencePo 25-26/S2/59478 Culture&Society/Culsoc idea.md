# Wearing the Imagined Community: Hanfu, Social Media, and the Embodied Nation in Contemporary China
[[Wearing the Imagined Community 迪奥马面裙事件与民族情绪分析]]
Jiatong LIU

## Problematic

In June 2022, French luxury brand Dior released a midi skirt strikingly similar to the mǎmiàn qún (马面裙，horse-face skirt), a traditional Han Chinese garment, without any acknowledgement of its cultural origins. The following debate on Weibo was swift and intense, with millions of users invoking the language of cultural sovereignty, national dignity, and historical memory. Yet this episode was a crystallization of an affective nationalism that had long been present within the Hanfu movement: a grassroots cultural revival centered on wearing traditional Han Chinese clothing, which emerged from internet subcultures in the early 2000s and has since grown into a mass youth phenomenon, closely intertwined with broader ulture alongside Lolita fashion and cosplay.

**This paper asks: how do participants in digital Hanfu communities construct, negotiate, and perform national identity through the embodied practice of wearing traditional clothing?** This question enters into dialogue with Benedict Anderson's foundational theory of nationalism in Imagined Communities (1983). Anderson argued that nations are imagined communities brought into being through print capitalism, by the shared, simultaneous reading of newspapers and novels producing a sense of horizontal solidarity among strangers. \

I argue that digital Hanfu communities reveal the limits of this framework. In the twenty-first century, national imaginaries are increasingly produced not through reading but through embodied, visual, and performative practices, such as wearing, photographing, posting, and commenting, which circulated across social media platforms. The medium has changed, and with it the logic of communal imagination: the body itself becomes the site through which the nation is collectively produced and affirmed. Anderson's framework requires revision to account for this shift from textual to embodied and digital modalities of national imagination.

Alexander's theory of social performance (The Meanings of Social Life, 2003) provides a second theoretical lens. Hanfu wearers and digital protesters during the Dior controversy are not merely expressing pre-existing nationalist sentiment, but actively performing national identity for a digital audience. Staging authenticity, collective commitment, and cultural legitimacy together mirror the performative logic Alexander identifies in social movements and political action.

Hebdige's concept of subcultural recuperation (1979) provides another theoretical lens. I consider whether the Hanfu movement's strong nationalist charge complicates the standard recuperation narrative, given that commercial incorporation does not necessarily neutralize a practice whose resistant potential is tied to national rather than class identity.

## Methodology

This paper employs two complementary qualitative methods.

-       First, a discourse analysis of Weibo content surrounding the 2022 Dior controversy, examining a selection of posts, comments, and key opinion leader statements to analyze how public discourse mobilized the mǎmiàn qún as a symbol of national identity and cultural ownership.

-       Second, a digital ethnography of Hanfu communities on Xiaohongshu and YouTube, with particular attention to how participants articulate their motivations, negotiate authenticity, and express nationalist sentiment in both domestic and diaspora contexts.

The latter offering a productive comparative angle on how national identity is performed when physically removed from the national territory.

## Preliminary hypothesis

I hypothesize that digital Hanfu communities constitute a form of what Billig (1995) calls banal nationalism. Banal nationalism refers to the everyday, unremarkable flagging of national identity through mundane cultural practices . But one that is periodically intensified into explicit nationalist mobilization by external provocations such as Western cultural appropriation. This suggests that Anderson's imagined community is not only textual but increasingly worn, photographed, and performed in digital space, requiring an updated account of how nations are imagined in the age of social media.
