🔑 **Context you need to know**: The 19th century (“Pax Britannica”) was relatively peaceful compared to earlier centuries, with Britain as a global hegemon. But wars were still possible, and financing was crucial—wars were expensive, and states often relied on bond markets.

## **Market Structure: Prestige and Certification**

Flandreau & Flores revisit Polanyi’s idea with modern economic theory.
- They argue that the **Rothschilds were not just lenders, but certifiers**: like rating agencies today.
- Certification by Rothschilds signaled that a borrower was trustworthy. This gave them natural monopoly power .

📌 **Extra knowledge**: This is similar to how _Moody’s or S&P_ function today. Their rating can drastically lower or raise borrowing costs. The Rothschilds had the same function, but informaly, through their prestige.

- **Prestige as sunk cost**: Rothschilds built their reputation over decades; to preserve it, they avoided reckless war loans .
- **Outcome**: They had leverage over states—if you wanted access to capital, you had to play by their peace-oriented rules.

## **Evidence: Markets Against War**

The authors use a large dataset of London bond issues (1815–1913)
- Out of 380 foreign loans, **only about 5% were issued during wars** .
- Major “wars between powers” (e.g., German & Italian unifications) saw **no London financing** .
- When loans _were_ made during wars, they often came with worse terms (higher yields, discounts), unless certified by Rothschilds .

## **Case Study: The Belgian Crisis of 1830–39**

- Belgium declared independence from the Netherlands in 1830, sparking fears of a European war.
- The Rothschilds refused to lend until diplomacy stabilized the situation.
- In 1831, Belgium secured a loan from Rothschilds with **Article 9**, which suspended lending if foreign intervention escalated beyond the London Conference’s mandate 

👉 Effect: Belgium could not drag France into war without losing financial support. The clause acted as a **peace-enforcing mechanism**.

📌 **Background you need to know**: The London Conference (1830–31) was a diplomatic summit of the great powers. Without finance, Belgium was forced to cooperate with the framework, not escalate.

## **Beyond Economics: Why This Matters**

This is not just about money—it’s about **power and governance without formal institutions**.
- The Rothschilds acted as an _informal international regulator_.
- They had no army, but their financial clout substituted for one.
- This system only worked because:
    - Markets were centralized in London.
    - Rothschilds had unrivaled prestige.
    - States were dependent on credit for both war and reconstruction.

📌 **Extra context**: This resembles today’s IMF conditionality. Countries in crisis can borrow—but only if they follow policy reforms. The Rothschilds played a proto-IMF role in the 19th century.

## **Limits and Critiques**

- **Markets didn’t always prevent war**: France in 1870 borrowed from lesser bankers (Morgans) to fight Prussia, though at worse terms .
- **Haute Finance could not stop all conflicts**: but they raised costs, creating disincentives.
- **Power dimension**: The Rothschilds acted in their own interest as much as in the interest of peace.
