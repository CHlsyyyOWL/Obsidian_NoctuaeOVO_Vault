# **1. 你的研究问题非常不错。但请你更明确地解释：为什么“Piaohao 为什么没有公司化”是一个重要的问题？它揭示什么更普遍的机制？**

  

### **建议回答：**

  

This question matters because the Piaohao represent the _most successful and most institutionally sophisticated_ form of indigenous finance in late imperial China. If even the most advanced native institutions could not corporatize, we must ask **whether the constraint was economic, institutional, or cultural**.

  

My findings suggest this is not an anomaly but a mechanism:

**delegation without codification.**

The Qing state delegated fiscal functions to the Piaohao but _never_ codified legal autonomy (no legal personhood, no limited liability, no shareholder rights).

  

This mechanism can generalize to other cases—guilds, lineage trusts, native banks—where **efficiency strengthens political incorporation instead of institutional autonomy**.

  

So this question helps us understand

→ why strong markets did not produce modern institutions

→ why China diverged institutionally despite commercial sophistication.

# **2. You rely heavily on Shiue & Keller (2007). Some scholars argue price convergence cannot fully capture “market integration.” How do you respond?**

  

### **建议回答：**

  

I agree that price convergence is an imperfect proxy, but it is the _best available cross-regional quantitative measure_ for the period. And importantly, I do not overclaim:

  

I do NOT say “China was the same as Europe”;

I say: **market efficiency alone did not produce institutional reform**.

  

Even if we adopt more cautious interpretations—e.g., “integration in grains but not in capital”—the paradox still holds:

**China had strong commercial coordination but weak institutional modernization.**

  

My argument is robust to alternative measures because the outcome—non-corporatization—does not change.

# **3. How does your study expand or challenge North’s theory of institutional change?**

  

### **建议回答：**

  

North emphasizes that falling transaction costs encourage institutional innovation.

But my findings show a **missing layer** between efficiency and reform:

  

### **➤** 

### **The cultural–political constraint layer.**

  

Efficiency was absorbed into the fiscal structure rather than translated into legal autonomy.

  

In short:

- **Germany**: efficiency → codification → corporate autonomy
    
- **China**: efficiency → political incorporation → moralized hierarchy
    

  

Thus I extend North by suggesting:

  

> “Institutional change requires not only efficiency but also the _decline_ of moralized authority.”

  

This is fully consistent with Pye’s idea of Primitive Power.

# **4. You argue Germany and China are both “authoritarian modernization.” But Bismarck’s state still allowed corporate autonomy. Why did the Qing not do the same?**

  

### **建议回答：**

  

Because the **logic of coordination** differed.

  

### **Germany:**

- State legitimacy came from _legal rationality_.
    
- State strengthened itself by building impersonal institutions.
    
- Universal banks were complementary to state-building.
    

  

### **China:**

- State legitimacy came from _moral authority_.
    
- Autonomous organizations threatened the symbolic unity of the empire.
    
- Piaohao were incorporated as moral appendages, not legal entities.
    

  

Thus identical political centralization produced _opposite_ outcomes because:

**Germany centralized law; China centralized morality.**

# **5. Explain “Primitive Power” and why it is essential to your argument.**

  

### **建议回答：**

  

Primitive Power (Pye 1988) means:

  

> A system where political legitimacy derives from moral hierarchy, not legal rationality.

  

In such a system:

- The ruler embodies moral order.
    
- Organizations exist _by grace of the state_.
    
- Autonomy is suspicious because it fragments moral unity.
    

  

This explains why:

- Limited liability was read as “逃信”—escaping moral debt.
    
- Corporate ownership was seen as “股非一家，东非一姓”—betraying lineage unity.
    
- Competition was immoral, not merely inefficient.
    

  

Primitive Power provides the **macro-cultural logic** linking all micro-level observations.

# **6. Could the Piaohao have corporatized earlier if foreign banks had come later?**

  

### **建议回答：**

  

Even without foreign banks, corporatization would still be unlikely because the constraints were:

- **Moral** (renqing, enqing, filial loyalty)
    
- **Organizational** (apprenticeship, guarantor networks)
    
- **Institutional** (lack of company law, lack of judicial enforcement)
    

  

Foreign banks _triggered_ the crisis but did not _cause_ the moral ceiling.

The ceiling existed long before foreign pressure emerged.

# **7. Why do you claim the Piaohao were “successful failures”?**

  

### **建议回答：**

  

They were successful because:

- They created China’s first nationwide financial network
    
- They handled the empire’s highest-stakes fiscal remittances
    
- They solved commitment problems without formal law
    

  

But they were failures because:

- The very moral mechanisms that ensured trust **prevented codification**
    
- Unlimited liability discouraged capital pooling
    
- Centralized M-form hierarchy became rigid as scale increased
    

  

Thus success created dependence, and dependence prevented modernization.

# **8. How do you know moral values mattered more than economic constraints? Could it simply be lack of capital or technology?**

  

### **建议回答：**

  

Because the empirical evidence shows:

- **Capital existed** (they managed millions of taels annually)
    
- **Technology existed** (telegraph, accounting, double-entry books)
    
- **Demand existed** (late Qing industrial loans, railway investments)
    

  

What was missing was **cultural legitimacy** of corporate separation.

  

Direct archival words show moral reasoning:

- Limited liability = 逃信
    
- Multiple shareholders = “东非一姓”
    
- Competition = immoral
    

  

Thus the **grammar of decision-making** was moral, not economic.

# **11. Some scholars argue that the Shanxi Piaohao were simply too small and too decentralized to corporatize. Isn’t your explanation overemphasizing culture?**

  

### **⭐ 建议回答：**

  

The small scale explanation does not hold, because by 1870 the Piaohao operated **over 500 branches across more than twenty provinces** and processed **5–6 million taels of fiscal transfers annually**.

This scale is comparable to medium universal banks in 19th-century Germany.

  

The reason scale didn’t translate into corporatization is precisely because:

- economic scale rested on **informal trust**,
    
- trust rested on **personal moral obligation**,
    
- moral obligation **cannot scale through law**.
    

  

Thus cultural factors are not an “overlay” but the **mechanism that converts scale into fragility**.

  

Scale grew, but _personhood remained the unit of credit_.

This is exactly what prevented legal depersonalization.

  

Culture explains **why scale did not institutionalize**.

# **12. Could it be that the Qing failed because it was a weak state, not a culturally moralizing state?**

  

### **⭐ 建议回答：**

  

The Qing was weak _fiscally_ but strong _morally_.

This is what Pye calls _Primitive Power_:

- low coercive capacity
    
- high normative authority
    

  

This duality explains the paradox:

  

> a state too weak to build institutions,

> but strong enough to prevent others from becoming autonomous.

  

The Qing couldn’t formalize corporatization,

but it could **morally delegitimize** it.

  

Thus “weak state capacity” and “moralized power” are not alternatives—they are **joint components** of the same governance logic.

# **13. Your argument about moral obligation is compelling, but how do you distinguish culture from simply rational risk aversion?**

  

### **⭐ 建议回答：**

  

Because the archival language is **not economic**, but **ethical**.

  

Partners say:

- “逃信” (escaping trust), not “risk is too high”
    
- “恩情还不完” (can never repay favors), not “limited liability is unclear”
    
- “股非一家” (shares without kin), not “ownership structure uncertain”
    

  

These are moral categories, not risk categories.

  

If managers were simply risk-averse, they would:

1. demand collateral,
    
2. demand state regulation,
    
3. demand clearer legal rights.
    

  

Instead, they rejected corporatization **because it violates virtue**, not because it increases risk.

  

Thus moral reasoning is the **primary causal mechanism**, not risk.

# **14. Why do you confidently reject the industrial-demand hypothesis? Could it be that China simply did not need corporate banks?**

  

### **⭐ 建议回答：**

  

If industrial demand were the decisive factor, then:

- the late Qing’s massive railway projects
    
- military modernization campaigns
    
- telegraph expansion
    
- provincial industrial ventures
    

  

should have generated demand for corporate banking.

  

In fact, the Piaohao _were repeatedly invited_ to engage in industrial financing (Xu 2008), but **declined**.

  

Thus the absence of corporatization was not from lack of demand—it was from:

- unlimited liability that made large loans impossible,
    
- filial moralism that stigmatized profit-driven expansion,
    
- political culture that kept finance tied to fiscal logistics.
    

  

Industrial demand existed; **institutional legitimacy did not**.

# **15. If Primitive Power is the key, why could Meiji Japan corporatize under a similar Confucian tradition?**

  

### **⭐ 建议回答：**

  

Japan shares Confucian elements, but it never developed **4,000 years of uninterrupted central absolutism**.

  

Three structural differences matter:

1. **Tokugawa polycentricity**
    
    Japan maintained strong regional daimyo autonomy; institutional competition existed.
    
2. **Meiji oligarchs adopted Western corporate law wholesale**, not incrementally.
    
3. **Samurai political culture valorized legal duties**, not moral reciprocity.
    
    Japan moralized _law_, China moralized _hierarchy_.
    

  

Thus cultural similarities do not imply institutional equivalence.

Primitive Power is not “Confucianism,” but **persistent political absorption of social authority**—and Japan did not have this in the same way.

# **16. Your methodology uses qualitative process tracing. How do you ensure causal inference and avoid narrative determinism?**

  

### **⭐ 建议回答：**

  

I ensure causal clarity through three strategies:

  

### **(1)** 

### **Diagnostic evidence**

  

Archival letters (e.g., Li Hongling correspondence) show actors explicitly using _moral_ categories to justify rejecting corporatization.

  

### **(2)** 

### **Causal sequencing**

- Market integration (18th c.)
    
    → Piaohao rise (1840s–70s)
    
    → Fiscal embedding (mid–late 19th c.)
    
    → Reform attempt (1908)
    
    → Failure (1912)
    

  

The failure cannot be explained by markets or scale; it follows **institutional and cultural path dependence**.

  

### **(3)** 

### **Shadow comparison**

  

Germany under authoritarianism did corporatize → showing politics alone doesn’t cause failure.

Japan corporatized → showing Confucian ethics alone don’t block modernity.

Thus China’s case reflects a distinct mechanism: **moralized absorption**.

  

This triangulation prevents narrative determinism.
