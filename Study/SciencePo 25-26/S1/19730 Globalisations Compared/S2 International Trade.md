# Hegemony

## The debate they enter

- Earlier **hegemonic stability theory (HST)** — Kindleberger, Krasner, Gilpin — argued that a **hegemon** (a state with preponderant power) supplies public goods (like free trade, stable currency) and uses its power (coercion, side-payments, or bargains) to induce others to liberalize.
- The “orthodox” view is that openness requires hegemonic leadership, often understood as **the “first face” of power**: direct inducements or coercion.
    
James & Lake critique this: they show **little evidence** that Britain forced others to open through threats or material side-payments (e.g. with the U.S. or the German Zollverein)

## 1st: Coercive Hegemony

**A coercive form,** where the Hegemon ‘actively manipulates the policies of other states In turn this can be based on: 
1. Economic sanctions, foreign or military aid, the monopoly to access to some strategic resource (rare earths, etc), etc. Here, J&L 
2. 2. Market power, which is the object of this paper 3. Ideas, ideologies, political representations, hence the capacity to ’control the agenda’. That is the ‘Gramscian’ dimension.

## 2nd: Benevolent Hegemony

**A benevolent form**, derived from the classic contribution by Economic historian Kindleberger
- the Hegemon provides free-of-charge a set of public goods that regulate the world, especially the world economy. Examples: peace (the British Navy, or NATO), a working intl monetary system (£or $-based, plus the IMF), an international trading regime (GATT/WTO), etc.

- They borrow from Bachrach & Baratz’s “two faces of power.”
- The **second face** of hegemony is **structural/market-based influence**:
    - By opening its own markets (e.g. Britain’s repeal of the Corn Laws), the hegemon **changes international prices** and hence reshapes the domestic payoff structure of groups in other countries.
    - These shifts empower pro-trade coalitions abroad (e.g. U.S. western grain farmers), which then lobby for and achieve trade liberalization domestically (the Walker Tariff of 1846).
- This is a subtler, “invisible hand” mechanism: instead of commanding or bribing, the hegemony **sets the external context** in which foreign domestic politics operates.
    
They write that Britain’s move was _“not simply altruistic,”_ but a strategic calculation that repealing the Corn Laws would **induce others to follow** by tipping their internal political balances.

## 3rd: Ideology Hegemony

- They also mention a **“third face of hegemony”**: ideational or normative influence.
    - Hegemons can shape the intellectual climate — e.g. free-trade ideology, legitimacy of liberal economics — so that policy preferences abroad are aligned without direct material pressure.
    
- In 1846, Britain’s moral and ideological defense of free trade (Manchester School, Cobden, Bright) amplified the structural price effects.
