# The State in Comparative Perspective 

- State:
	- The terminology itself includes multiple meanings, including the pre-modern ones and the modern nations. 
	- The world also has state-less societies

- Definition of **Modern state:**
	- Human community that successfully claims the monopoly of the legitimate use of physical force within a given territory.-  Max Weber
	- A state is an organisation with a comparative advantage in violence, extending over a geographic area whose boundaries are determined by its own power to tax constituents. - North
	- States are relatively centralised, differentiated organizations, the officials of which, more or less, successfully claim control over the chief concentrated means of violence with in a population inhabiting a lagrge contiguous territory. - Tilly
- Similarity and Differences:
	- Similarity: contains violence in a certain territory
	- Difference: Legitimacy of Violence

- Other Definitions:
	- **Sovereignty**: The state holds within its territory ultimate authority. Each state recognise no power superior to itself. It engages in political activity on nobody's mandate but its own, commits to its resources of its own, operate under its own steam. 
	- **Nation**: a group of people who share some sort of common identity like a language, a religion, an ethnicity, or a shared history (Nation-State)
	- **Government**: States have governments with the recognized authority to administer and to represent the state in dealing with other governments. 
	- **Failed State**: A failed state-like entity that cannot coerce and is unable to successfully control the inhabitants of a given territory.

- Methodological Contribution: Against Evolution & Ethnocentrism

> [!note]- Video： The Art of Not Being Governed - Why people will deliberately remain stateless?
> Research Area: 
> Scott focuses on “Zomia” — the vast highland region stretching from northern Vietnam, Laos, northern Thailand, Burma, and northeastern India to southwestern China (Yunnan, Guizhou). This zone has long been home to large numbers of stateless societies.
> 
> Research Method: 
> Literature research + anthropological sources + ethnographic materials from international organizations working with groups such as the Miao/Hmong and other minorities. Scott does not primarily rely on fieldwork but on cross-disciplinary comparative research.
> 
> Findings:
> 
> Shatter Zone
> 	•	The formation of highland populations is not a natural survival but the result of pressure from states (especially slavery, taxation, and coercion of labor).
> 	•	The highlands thus became a refuge of the oppressed, a zone of shattered peoples composed of diverse ethnic, linguistic, and cultural groups.
> 
> Slash-and-Burn Agriculture
> 	•	Fired zone agriculture (slash-and-burn) is an intentional choice, not a mark of “primitiveness.”
> 	•	Scott emphasizes that it is a form of “state-repelling agriculture”:
> 	•	Dispersed crops (difficult to appropriate),
> 	•	Shifting fields (hard to fix and register),
> 	•	Variable yields (unsuitable for state taxation).
> 	•	Thus, swidden is not merely a survival strategy but a deliberate, institutionalized technology of evasion.
> 
> Valleys to Hills
> 	•	Populations chose to leave the valleys to escape taxation and state regulation.
> 	•	Highland societies are characterized by: no written scripts (harder to record and control), no centralized authority, and an emphasis on moral communities rather than codified state law.
> 	•	For weak groups, life in a moral community offers more advantages than being governed by the state.
> 	•	Valleys are the natural bases of states because:
> 	•	Wet-rice cultivation yields high surpluses → easy to appropriate;
> 	•	Valleys provide transport corridors → easy to control militarily.
> 	•	Migration to the hills was not “a regression to primitivism,” but a conscious geographical strategy of state evasion.
> 
> 
> Moral Culture
> 	•	Life in moral communities favors the weak.
> 	•	Upland societies emphasize egalitarianism, oral tradition, and decentralized social organization — cultural features deliberately designed to keep state power at bay.
> 	•	For example:
> 	•	No writing (avoiding inscription and state legibility),
> 	•	Oral history (rewritable, resistant to official historiography),
> 	•	Fragmented politics (preventing the emergence of a centralized authority that could be co-opted by states).
> 
> The Modern State’s Penetration
> 	•	Modern technologies (e.g. helicopters) enable the state to extend its reach into upland zones.
> 	•	Scott stresses that “the last refuges are disappearing” as states incorporate uplands through:
> 	•	Frontier militarization,
> 	•	Road building,
> 	•	Satellite surveillance,
> 	•	NGO and international interventions.
> 	•	The uplands are being drawn, often forcibly, into state space.
> 
> 
> 
> Scott’s Larger Theoretical Contribution
> 	•	He challenges the conventional view that statelessness = backwardness, arguing instead that it represents a political choice.
> 	•	He proposes that the “art of not being governed” is not only an economic or geographical strategy but also a cultural, identity, and historiographical strategy.
> 	•	He explicitly links this to his earlier work Seeing Like a State (1998): states govern by creating legibility, while hill societies deliberately generate opacity to resist incorporation.

> 研究对象与方法
> • 研究区域：Scott 聚焦于 “Zomia” ——指从越南北部、老挝、泰国北部、缅甸、印度东北到中国西南（云南、贵州）的广阔高地地区。这个区域长期以来存在大量“无国家社会”。
> • 研究方法：文献研究 + 人类学资料 + 国际组织与民族志材料（涉及苗族、蒙古族等边缘群体）。Scott 并不主要依靠实地田野，而是通过跨学科的比较研究。
> 
> 破碎地带 (Shatter Zone)
> • 高地人口的形成并非自然遗留，而是由低地国家的压力（特别是奴隶掠夺、税收、徭役）所驱赶。
> • 因此高地社会是被压迫者的避难所，包含了不同民族、语言、文化的混合体。
> 
> 关于“刀耕火种农业”
> • 刀耕火种是一种有意选择，而非“原始落后”。
> • Scott 强调这是 一种“反国家的农业”：
> • 作物分散（难以征收），
> • 土地轮换（难以固定），
> • 产量不稳定（不利于国家征粮）。
> • 因此它不仅是“逃避策略”，而且构成了 制度化的抗国家技术。
> 
> 关于“山谷 vs. 山地”
> • 选择离开山谷逃避税收与国家规制
> 山地社会的特征：没有文字（难以记录和控制）、没有中央权威、强调“道德文化”而非国家法律。
> • 对弱势群体来说，生活在这种“无国家的道德共同体”比被国家统治更有优势。
> • 山谷（河谷）是国家的天然根据地，因为：
> • 河谷适合水稻种植 → 产量高 → 易于集中征收。
> • 河谷交通便利 → 易于军事控制。
> • 因此，迁往山地并不是“向落后迁移”，而是刻意逃避国家控制的地理策略。
> 
> 关于“道德文化”
> • 弱者生活在道德文化里更有优势。
> • 山地社会强调平等主义、口述传统、去中心化的社会关系，这些文化特征本身就是为了避免国家权力渗透。
> • 比如：
> • 没有文字（避免被国家记录/控制）；
> • 口述历史（可随时改写，逃避国家正史）；
> • 分散政治（避免出现容易被收编的中央权威）。
> 
> 关于“现代国家的进入”
> • 现代科技如直升机使国家能够覆盖这些地区。
> • Scott 强调 “最后的避难所正在消失”，现代国家通过：
> • 边疆军事化，
> • 公路修建，
> • 卫星监控，
> • NGO/国际组织介入，
> 已经把高地逐渐纳入版图。
> 
> Scott 的更大理论意义：
> 1. 他挑战了“无国家 = 落后”的传统观念，强调这是政治选择。
> 2. 他提出 “逃避国家的艺术” 不仅是经济和地理策略，更是 文化、身份与历史书写的策略。
> 3. 他呼应了自己在《Seeing Like a State》里的观点：国家治理依赖 可读性 (legibility)，而山地社会则刻意制造“不透明”。

	- Emergence of Law Merchants in medieval Europe to ensure the protection of contracts
		- Law merchants have a kind of coercive power to ensure the trade. 
	- From reputation to institution
		- Reputation serves as a guarantee
		- To Institutions, Traders pay the Institution for a guarantee. 
	- Security guarantees by local lords

- Legibility
	- Understandable, easy to manage with statistics and control. 
	- "Suddenly, processes as disparate as the creation of permanent last names, the standardization of weights and measures, the establishment of cadastral surveys and population registers, the invention of freehold tenure, the standardization of language and legal discourse, the design of cities, and the organization of transportation seemed comprehensible as attempts at **legibility and simplification**. In each case, officials took exceptionally complex, illegible, and local social practices, such as land tenure customs or naming customs, and created a standard grid whereby it could be centrally recorded and monitored."
