# Reading

## Summary

### Harriet Martineau
•	An English writer, journalist, and social theorist.
•	She is often regarded as one of the earliest female sociologists and sometimes called the “first female sociologist.”
•	Known for making political economy and social philosophy accessible to the wider public, especially through her Illustrations of Political Economy (1832–34), where she turned abstract economic theory into popular stories.
•	Martineau combined a commitment to empiricism, a belief in the usefulness of science to improve society, and a sharp sensitivity to gender, class, and cultural inequalities.
•	Politically, she leaned toward progressive liberalism, supporting the abolition of slavery, women’s rights, and social reforms.

### How to Observe Morals and Manners (1838)

This work is often considered her most “sociological” text, predating Durkheim and Weber by several decades. It can be read as an early methodological manual for social observation, something like a precursor to ethnography.

Key Themes:
	1.	Purpose of Social Observation
		•	Martineau argued that travelers and observers could contribute to knowledge by systematically studying the morals and manners of societies, rather than simply offering personal impressions or exotic tales.
		•	She sought to transform “travel writing” into a scientific and comparative tool.
	2.	Distinction between “Morals” and “Manners”
		•	Morals: the underlying values, norms, and principles that structure a society — e.g., justice, equality, liberty.
		•	Manners: the outward expressions, customs, and everyday practices that give shape to those morals in social life.
		•	Together, they form the social fabric that must be observed and analyzed.
	3.	Methodological Concerns
		•	Observers should avoid bias, especially ethnocentrism, and be aware of their own prejudices.
		•	Importance of systematic comparison across societies.
		•	Emphasis on empirical observation over mere speculation.
	4.	Gender and Marginality
		•	Martineau highlighted how women, enslaved people, and marginalized groups are crucial to understanding a society’s true moral condition.
		•	For example, she argued that the treatment of women is a key indicator of a society’s moral progress.
	5.	Political and Practical Implications
		•	She wanted social observation to feed into policy reform and social improvement.
		•	Thus, the book is not just descriptive but also normative: societies should be judged by universal principles of justice and equality.

Why it matters
	•	How to Observe Morals and Manners anticipates later debates in sociology and anthropology about objectivity, ethnography, and cultural relativism.
	•	It provides one of the earliest systematic methodological reflections on how to study society empirically.
	•	Martineau’s insistence on looking at gender and power relations makes her especially relevant to feminist sociology.

### Research Question, Answer, and Thinos

#### Research Question

Martineau is asking:

- How can we systematically and scientifically observe societies?
- What is the relationship between a society’s underlying moral values (morals) and its everyday social practices (manners)?
- How can a traveler or outsider make valid, unbiased judgments about another society?
    
In short, she’s trying to transform travel writing and casual observation into a **proto-sociological method**.

#### Methods

Martineau lays out **guidelines for social observation**, which resemble early ethnographic principles:

- **Systematic comparison**: Observers should compare societies carefully, not just describe them.
- **Avoiding bias**: She warns against ethnocentrism, prejudice, or moral superiority.
- **Everyday life as data**: Instead of only focusing on elites or institutions, look at ordinary practices (e.g., family relations, gender norms, rituals).
- **Indicators of morality**: She insists that the status of women, children, the poor, and enslaved people are _the truest measures of a society’s moral progress_.
- **Interdisciplinarity**: She brings together philosophy, political economy, and moral philosophy.
    
#### Main Results / Conclusions

- _Observation must be both empirical and moral_: you cannot separate description from evaluation.
- _Morals and manners are inseparable_: to understand a society, you need to see how abstract values manifest in daily practices.
- _Social progress can be measured_: by how inclusive and just a society is toward its weakest members.
- _Scientific objectivity is possible but limited_: the observer must acknowledge their own standpoint.
    
So the “result” is not a dataset or hypothesis test, but a **methodological framework** that anticipates sociology and anthropology.

---

When engaging critically with Martineau, you might reflect on:

- She claims observers must be impartial, but she herself embeds strong liberal and abolitionist values. _Can social science ever be free from normative commitments?_
- Martineau uses travelers as data-gatherers. _Does this introduce problems of representation (outsider bias)? How would her method differ from participant observation in anthropology later on?_
- Her criterion of social progress is the condition of women and the oppressed. _Is this a universal benchmark, or is it rooted in her own 19th-century liberal worldview?_

#### **Religion**

- Observe the **dominant faith** and its institutions.
    
- Look at **rituals, ceremonies, and clergy** as expressions of belief.
    
- Consider how religion influences **laws, education, and everyday practices**.
    
- Pay attention to how dissenters, unbelievers, or minority religions are treated.
    
#### **State / Government**

- Study the **form of government** (monarchy, democracy, etc.).
    
- Observe how authority is exercised and how people **respond to laws**.
    
- Examine **taxation, public expenditure, police, and military power**.
    
- Focus on how the state treats **citizens of different classes, genders, or races**.
    
#### **Domestic Life (Family)**

- The **status of women**: Martineau famously says that women’s condition is one of the best indicators of a society’s moral progress.
    
- Look at **marriage, divorce, child-rearing, inheritance**.
    
- Domestic relations reveal a society’s deeper moral code.
    
#### **Education**

- What is taught, who has access, and how it is organized.
    
- Observe **both formal schooling and informal training** (apprenticeships, moral instruction).
    
- How are children socialized into societal values?
    

#### **Class Relations / Economy**

- Daily work practices, distribution of wealth, living conditions.
    
- Observe treatment of the **poor, laborers, and enslaved persons**.
    
- Look at **manners of consumption**—what people buy, wear, eat—as reflections of values.
    

#### **Everyday Social Manners**

- Hospitality, greetings, treatment of strangers.
    
- Customs around **birth, marriage, death**.
    
- Public amusements, festivals, and social gatherings.
    
- These everyday practices reveal the “surface” of morals.
    
#### **Underlying Method**

- Martineau insists on **systematic and comparative observation**, not just anecdotes.
    
- She urges observers to see **how morals (values) and manners (customs) are consistent or inconsistent**.
    
    - Example: A society may proclaim “liberty” but enslave people — a contradiction that observation should reveal.
    
#### **Takeaway**
In _What to Observe_, Martineau basically builds a **19th-century fieldwork guide**. She wants observers to:

1. Look beyond appearances.
    
2. Compare institutions systematically.
    
3. Treat **gender relations, treatment of minorities, and the condition of the weak** as key diagnostic indicators of morality.
    
This is why she is often called a **proto-sociologist**: she’s already telling us what data count in social science.

### Part 3: Mechanical Methods

Martineau asks: _What practical, bodily, and intellectual qualities must a traveler–observer have in order to succeed in social observation?_ She lists several “requisites,” which together form a checklist of what we might today call “fieldwork skills.”

#### **Health and Physical Endurance**

- The observer needs a **sound body**: strength to travel, endure climate, and bear fatigue.
    
- Ill health will cloud judgment and limit the ability to see clearly.
#### **Good Temper and Patience**

- To be received well, the observer must be **amiable, patient, and even-tempered**.
    
- A harsh or arrogant demeanor will distort what people show you.

#### **Firmness and Courage**

- Courage to face difficulties or dangers while traveling.
    
- Also, **moral firmness**: not being swayed by first impressions or local prejudices.

#### **Industry and Method**

- The observer should be **systematic**, taking careful notes, collecting facts diligently.
    
- Random or lazy observation produces only anecdotes, not reliable social knowledge.
    
#### **Humility**

- Avoid assuming superiority over the society being observed.
    
- Recognize one’s own limits of knowledge.
---
#### **Why Martineau Calls Them “Mechanical”**

- She distinguishes these **practical, bodily, and temperamental conditions** from the higher intellectual requirements of theory and philosophy.
    
- These are “mechanical” in the sense of **basic tools of observation** — like the equipment of a scientist.
    
##### **Broader Meaning**
This section may sound like advice for travelers, but it anticipates **fieldwork methodology**:

- Physical and moral preparedness.
    
- Awareness of how the observer’s presence and behavior shape the quality of data.
    
- The need for **discipline, patience, and reflexivity** in social inquiry.

> [!note]- Positive Philosophy
> 一、背景与总体定位
> 	•	奥古斯特·孔德 (Auguste Comte, 1798–1857)：法国哲学家，被认为是社会学的奠基人之一。
> 	•	《实证哲学课程》(Cours de philosophie positive, 1830–1842)：六卷本著作，是孔德最重要的代表作。
> 	•	其目标：建立一种系统的“科学的社会学”，摆脱形而上学与神学，以经验和观察为基础来研究社会。
> 
> ⸻
> 
> 二、核心内容
> 	1.	三阶段法则 (Law of Three Stages)
> 	•	神学阶段 (theological stage)：以神灵解释自然与社会（幼年期）。
> 	•	形而上学阶段 (metaphysical stage)：以抽象概念、权利、自然法则来解释现象（青春期）。
> 	•	实证阶段 (positive stage)：以科学方法观察、归纳、建立规律（成年期）。
> 👉 社会学必须进入“实证阶段”才能成为真正的科学。
> 	2.	科学层级论 (Hierarchy of Sciences)
> 	•	数学 → 天文学 → 物理学 → 化学 → 生物学 → 社会学
> 	•	孔德认为社会学应当是“最后一门科学”，建立在其他学科的成果之上。
> 	•	社会学的独特性：研究社会秩序与社会进步。
> 	3.	社会学的两个分支
> 	•	社会静力学 (social statics)：研究社会秩序、制度、稳定。
> 	•	社会动力学 (social dynamics)：研究社会变迁、历史发展。
> 👉 他强调“秩序与进步”的统一。
> 	4.	宗教与道德功能
> 	•	孔德晚期提出“人类宗教”（Religion of Humanity），试图用道德与科学来替代传统宗教。
> 	•	在学科体系中，他赋予社会学一种“道德引导”的地位。
> 
> ⸻
> 
> 三、在社会学中的意义
> 	•	将社会学确立为一门独立科学。
> 	•	强调观察、实验、比较、历史方法。
> 	•	引导后世社会学家（如涂尔干、斯宾塞）在社会事实与社会规律的研究中延续“科学化”的路径。
> 	•	但也受到批评：过于线性、欧洲中心主义、忽视了社会中的冲突与多样性。
> 
> ⸻
> 
> 四、课堂讨论切入点
> 	1.	孔德如何将“自然科学模式”应用到社会？
> 	2.	他的“三阶段法则”是否过于决定论？
> 	3.	为什么他强调“社会学是科学的顶点”？这与当时的法国社会背景（大革命后秩序重建）有什么联系？
> 	4.	与当代社会学的多元研究路径（符号互动论、批判理论、后殖民研究）相比，他的实证主义还具有什么启发和局限？

> [!note]- Aristocracy
> Social Stratification: Absence of Aristocracy
> 
> 1. Institutional and Ideological Reasons
> 	•	In aristocratic societies (e.g. feudal Europe), class hierarchy was institutionally fixed (nobility vs. commoners) and ideologically justified (divine right, hereditary privilege).
> 	•	In societies that rejected aristocracy (e.g. revolutionary America, republican France), institutions and ideology promoted equality of legal status.
> 	•	Consequence: there is no rigid hereditary nobility. Stratification has to find other bases (wealth, education, status symbols).
> 
> ⸻
> 
> 2. Emergence of a Pseudo-Aristocracy
> 	•	Even without hereditary nobility, elites reappear in different forms — an aristocracy of wealth, education, or office.
> 	•	This “pseudo-aristocracy” imitates aristocratic functions (exclusive clubs, elite schools, inherited networks), but it lacks the old legitimizing ideology.
> 	•	It tends to be more unstable: mobility is possible, but elites still guard boundaries.
> 
> ⸻
> 
> 3. Status-Consciousness and Conformism
> 	•	In the absence of fixed aristocratic ranks, people become highly sensitive to relative status (who is richer, more refined, more connected).
> 	•	This fosters conformism: individuals imitate social codes of the group just above them to signal belonging.
> 	•	Tocqueville warned that in democratic societies, people become anxious about their place, producing restless comparison.
> 
> ⸻
> 
> 4. Mercenary Behavior
> 	•	If nobility once valued honor and tradition, the new pseudo-aristocracy tends to value money, utility, and personal advancement.
> 	•	“Mercenary behavior” here means: pursuing wealth and advantage in a transactional way, since social position is less secure and must be constantly defended.
> 	•	This is linked to instrumental rationality: relationships and behaviors are judged by what they bring materially.
> 
> ⸻
> 
> In Short
> 	•	Without aristocracy, stratification doesn’t vanish — it shifts from hereditary and stable to fluid but anxious.
> 	•	Consequences:
> 	•	Emergence of new elites (pseudo-aristocracy).
> 	•	Individuals become status-conscious and conformist.
> 	•	Behavior becomes more mercenary and utilitarian, as people seek to climb or secure their position.
