# Chat
🔍 Summary of What is Political Philosophy? by Leo Strauss

Leo Strauss offers a rigorous account of what political philosophy is, contrasting it with both positivist social science and historicism, and grounding it in the classical tradition of Plato and Aristotle.

1. Political Action and the Good
	•	All political action presupposes a distinction between better and worse—that is, it assumes some idea of the good.
	•	Political philosophy emerges when we explicitly seek knowledge of the good life and the good society, instead of simply operating on opinion.
	•	Thus, political philosophy is inherently normative: it is a quest for truth about the good society.

2. Philosophy vs. Positivism and Historicism
	•	Strauss critiques positivism, which seeks only factual knowledge and claims to be “value-free.” He argues that this neutrality often results in moral nihilism, or at least conformism and philistinism.
	•	He also criticizes historicism, which holds that all values are **historically contingent**. For historicists, the question of the good society loses its universality and urgency.
	•	For Strauss, both approaches evade the fundamental questions that political philosophy must face.

3. Political Philosophy and the Nature of the Political
	•	Political philosophy is concerned with the nature of political things, which are not neutral, but inherently evaluative: they require judgment in terms of good and bad, just and unjust.
	•	Unlike other arts (e.g., cooking or shepherding), politics does not have an agreed-upon end. The “common good” is contested, and this contestation demands philosophical inquiry.

4. The Classical Solution: Plato and Aristotle
	•	The classical tradition (especially Plato’s Laws and Aristotle’s Politics) offers a “natural” or “non-traditional” approach to political life.
	•	The classical concern is with the regime (politeia), which expresses the fundamental moral and institutional structure of society.
	•	The best regime, the telos of political life, aligns the good man with the good citizen. But such regimes are rare and often unactualized.

5. Moderation, Eros, and Philosophy
	•	Philosophy, in Strauss’s view, is not the possession of truth but the pursuit of it—animated by eros (desire).
	•	Political philosophy, then, is the moderation of the philosopher, who must engage with citizens, laws, and regimes while retaining a quest for higher truth.


Is political theory anchored towards theorizing “the good”—that is, theorizing models of government that will be conducive to the good? Or is political theory about describing what politics actually is—what its role is, what its scope is, etc?

🟡 Strauss’s Answer: Both—but Primarily the Former

1. Political Philosophy Is About the Good

Yes, political philosophy is fundamentally normative: it is anchored in the pursuit of the good, especially the good society or the best regime.
	•	All political action presupposes opinions about the good; political philosophy attempts to transform these opinions into knowledge.
	•	The central question of classical political philosophy is: What is the best regime? This question implies an evaluative standard.
	•	Therefore, theorizing the good—justice, virtue, the common good—is essential to Strauss’s conception of political philosophy.

2. But It Must Also Describe What Politics Is

However, political philosophy also entails descriptive work:
	•	It seeks to understand the nature of political things, which requires us to know what “political” means—what distinguishes it from other forms of human activity.
	•	Understanding regimes, laws, and political structures as they are is essential, but always in light of normative concerns.

3. Strauss’s Critique of “Descriptive-Only” Approaches
	•	Positivist social science tries to describe politics without normative evaluation, but Strauss argues that this is impossible and dangerous—it leads to nihilism or conformism.
	•	Historicism relativizes all normative standards, leading to a paralysis of judgment.

Thus, for Strauss, political theory must begin with descriptions but culminate in normative evaluation. Its telos is knowledge of the good society.
