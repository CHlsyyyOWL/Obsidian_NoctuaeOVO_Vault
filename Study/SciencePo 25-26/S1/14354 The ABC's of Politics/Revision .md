### AN ALTERNATIVE CONCEPTION OF JUSTICE 

- Nozick’s theory  based on three principles: 
	- **Justice of acquisition**:
		- Locke's theory of acquisition
		- “A person who acquires a holding in accordance with the principle of justice in acquisition is entitled to that holding” 
		- Does not worsen the situation of others
			- Cannot hold the only water hole in the community
	- **Justice of transfer**
		- “A person who acquires a holding in accordance with the principle of justice in transfer, from someone else entitled to the holding, is entitled to the holding” 
	- **Rectification of injustice**
		- “No one is entitled to a holding except by (repeated) applications of 1 and 2” 
- Decision difference: 
	- For Rawls, distribution is just if it fits the structure of just distribution => structural conception of justice 
	- For Nozick, distribution is just if it arises from another distribution by legitimate means => historical conception of justice

#### Critiques
- The problem of benign domination 
	- Pettit defines domination as **being subject to another’s arbitrary power**, even if that power is never exercised.
	- Paley argues that this definition is **too broad**:
		- A benevolent employer who would never exploit workers
		- A caring parent who never abuses authority
		- A welfare state that never arbitrarily interferes
	Under Pettit’s account, **all these count as domination** simply because they involve the _possibility_ of arbitrary interference.

- The problem of over-demandingnes
	- Pettit’s model **demands too much** from both institutions and citizens.
	- Reasons:
		- High institutional costs
		- Citizens must engage constantly in public scrutiny
		- Too much democratic participation for ordinary people with limited time/resources
		- Non-domination becomes _normatively desirable but practically unattainable_

- The problem of state domination
	- By empowering the state to prevent domination, Pettit unintentionally creates a **powerful state** capable of dominating citizens itself.
		- To prevent private domination, Pettit relies on public power.
		- But public power can become a new, even more dangerous, source of domination.
			- constrain employers
			- regulate families
			- control market power
			- prevent private domination
			- maintain rule of law

	**Who prevents the state—the guardian against domination—from becoming the dominator?**

## 1. Central Claim: From Social Contract to Racial Contract

Modern political modernity is not really founded on a universal, egalitarian social contract among free and equal individuals, but on a historically real **racial contract** that structures global white supremacy. The racial contract is political, moral, and epistemic, and it silently underlies classical and contemporary social contract theory. [Mills book][S9 PPT]

中文解释：传统社会契约说假装“人人平等签约”，Mills 说真正的“原初契约”是白人之间的种族契约，用来组织和正当化白人对非白人的统治。

  
“The Racial Contract is that set of formal or informal agreements…which categorizes the population into ‘white’ and ‘nonwhite’.” [Mills book]

“White supremacy is the unnamed political system that has made the modern world what it is today.” [Mills book]

S9 概括：“Paradox: political modernity shaped by white supremacy but barely mentioned by political philosophy; explanation → meta-contract = racial contract.” [S9 PPT]

  
**Assumptions (Classical Social Contract vs Mills)**
Classical social contract (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Kant):
- Assumes a pre-political state of nature with equal individuals.
- Assumes a universal capacity for reason and moral personality.
- Politics emerges from a voluntary agreement among all

**Mills’ counter-assumptions:**
- The “individuals” implicitly presupposed are **white, European, male, propertied**
- Nonwhites are not part of the contracting parties; they are positioned as objects of the contract rather than subjects.
- The actual historical process of state formation is colonial conquest, slavery, and racial domination; the “contract” is **racialized from the start**. [Mills book][S9 PPT]
    

1. Historically, the rise of the “golden age of contractualism” (1650–1800) is synchronized with European expansion, slavery, and colonial empire. [S9 PPT]
2. Political and legal documents (slave codes, colonial charters, naturalization laws) systematically distinguish between whites and nonwhites as different political and moral categories. [Mills book][S9 PPT]
3. Modern racial hierarchies and global North–South inequalities persist even after formal decolonization and civil rights reforms → the racial contract continues as an “informal racial contract.” [S9 PPT]
    
###  Critique 
Mills thus **politicizes** social contract theory: instead of a purely normative device, it becomes a theory that must account for historically real domination. The racial contract shows that liberalism’s apparent universality is a **myth that hides its racial foundations**.

中文总结：社会契约传统讲的是抽象平等个体如何签约；种族契约揭示的是：事实上“契约”只在白人之间成立，而非白人在这个结构里被默认为“次等人”“前政治者”。

  

## 2. Ontological/Epistemological Status of the Racial Contract

The racial contract is at once **real** (material, institutional) and **ideational** (discursive, symbolic, epistemic). To criticize it requires both empirical and philosophical critique. [S9 PPT]

“The Racial Contract is a political, moral, and epistemological pact.” [Mills book]

S9：“Two sides: racial contract is real → white supremacy has a material basis; racial contract is ideational → a type of discourse, representation.” [S9 PPT]

**Assumptions**

Realist assumption: white supremacy is not just prejudice but a **systemic structure of domination** embedded in law, economy, territory, and institutions.

Constructivist assumption: race and whiteness are historically invented categories, stabilized via discourse, knowledge production, and representation (“race as discursive system” per Stuart Hall). [S9 PPT]
- Material: slavery, segregation, colonial labor regimes, global division of labor → durable racialized inequalities. [S9 PPT]
    
- Ideational: science (racial biology, anthropology), religion, and philosophy generate and justify racial classification. Early racial knowledge was religious, later “scientific.” [S9 PPT]
    
- Therefore, the racial contract must be challenged both as a **set of institutions** and as an **epistemology of ignorance** (structured ways of not knowing). [Mills book]
    

**Critique**

By insisting on both sides, Mills avoids two errors:
1. Purely discursive theories that ignore the materiality of race.
2. Purely economic/materialist accounts that ignore how ideas and knowledge actively sustain domination.
    中文：种族契约既是“真”的制度事实，又是“虚”的文化叙事；批判必须同时拆解权力结构与认知结构。
    

## 3. Genealogy of Race and the First Period of the Racial Contract

Race is not a timeless biological fact but a **historically produced category** that emerges with European expansion (15th–19/20th c.). The first period of the racial contract coincides with “discovery” of the New World, slavery, genocide of indigenous populations, and colonialism in Asia and Africa. [S9 PPT]

S9：“Discovery of the ‘New World’; development of slavery; genocide of Native populations; expansion of colonialism; invention of race as ‘discursive system’.” [S9 PPT]

Mills: Europeans situate nonwhite peoples in the “state of nature” and see themselves as fully civilized persons. [Mills book]

 **Assumptions**
- Modern racial categories are constructed through **power** (process of racialization), not discovered as neutral scientific facts.
- Political identity (who can be a citizen) becomes tied to race, especially in the US (Naturalization Act of 1790, formal racial restrictions on naturalization until 1952). [S9 PPT]

 **Argument**
- Religion initially provides the framework (heathens vs Christians); over time, “scientific racism” replaces theological frameworks but keeps the hierarchy.
- “Who is white?” becomes a central political and legal question. The changing status of the Irish exemplifies how whiteness is **negotiated**, not fixed: Irish immigrants were gradually incorporated into whiteness. [S9 PPT]
**Critique**

This genealogy shows that race should be analyzed as **historical and political**, not biological. The liberal claim that racial hierarchy is an accidental deviation from its core principles is undermined: race is constitutive of the modern polity, not an external contamination.

## 4. Second Period: The “Informal Racial Contract”

After formal abolition of slavery and de jure segregation, the racial contract persists as an **informal racial contract**: a discrepancy between formal equality and real racial inequality, both domestically and globally. [S9 PPT]

S9: “Discrepancy between formal and real equality; internal: persistence of racial inequalities; external: effects of colonization still present in the international division of labour.” [S9 PPT]

**Assumptions**
- Legal equality does not automatically dismantle underlying structures of domination.
- Racial hierarchy is reproduced through practices, institutions, and economic structures even when explicit racial laws are removed

- In the US: ongoing disparities in income, incarceration, housing, and education reveal the endurance of racial hierarchy post–Civil Rights era.
- Globally: colonial patterns persist in North–South trade, resource extraction, and labor relations; former colonies occupy subordinate positions in the world economy.
    
**Critique**

The idea of an “informal racial contract” explains why “color-blind” liberalism and purely juridical reforms are insufficient. They address formal norms but not the underlying contract that structures racialized advantages and disadvantages.

  

## 5. Internal Logic of the Racial Contract (Mechanisms)

### **5.1 Anthropological Dimension**

  

Viewpoint: The racial contract creates a basic **ontological divide** between whites and nonwhites. [S9 PPT]

Evidence: White = civilization, reason, culture; nonwhite = nature, irrationality, savagery. [S9 PPT]

Assumption: Western anthropology and philosophy naturalize these binaries, turning contingent power relations into “facts” about human nature.

Argument: This anthropological coding justifies different treatment: whites as full persons, nonwhites as subpersons to be ruled, educated, or exploited. [Mills book]

Critique: Shows how Enlightenment notions of “Man” are racially coded; challenges the universality of the category “human.”

  

### **5.2 Spatial Dimension**

  

Viewpoint: The racial contract works through a **spatial divide**: civilized vs wild spaces, center vs periphery, metropolis vs colony, white suburbs vs racialized “urban jungles.” [S9 PPT]

Evidence: Logic of segregation; example: in France, slavery formally abolished only in 1794; colonial territories long treated as outside the full rights of the Republic. [S9 PPT]

Assumptions: Space is racialized; legal and policing regimes differ by space (colonies, reservations, ghettos).

Argument: Spatial segregation allows the racial contract to coexist with official universalist discourse: equality inside the white civic space, exception outside.

Critique: Links Foucaultian ideas of space/discipline to race; reveals that “neutral” urban planning often reproduces racial hierarchy.

  

### **5.3 Economic Dimension**

  

Viewpoint: The racial contract establishes an **extractive economic order**: racialized labor exploitation and resource extraction. [S9 PPT]

Evidence: S9 引用 Pomeranz 的 “Great Divergence”：欧洲财富积累依赖于殖民地的土地、劳力、资源。 [S9 PPT]

Assumptions: White working class can receive “national preferences” (welfare, labor protections) built on the super-exploitation of racialized labor, both internally and globally. [S9 PPT]

Argument: White supremacy and capitalism are intertwined; racial contract functions as an enabling condition for European/North Atlantic economic dominance.

Critique: Challenges purely class-based Marxist accounts that ignore race; shows how social democracy in the core countries is compatible with imperial exploitation of racialized others.

  

### **5.4 Epistemic Dimension**

  

Viewpoint: The racial contract produces an **“epistemology of ignorance”**: systematic white misunderstanding, misrepresentation, and denial. [Mills book]

Evidence: S9：“Racial contract is ‘invisible’ / ‘natural’; white privilege = ability to be unaware of race (McIntosh); ‘double consciousness’ (W. E. B. Du Bois).” [S9 PPT]

Assumptions: Knowledge and perception are shaped by social position; dominant groups are motivated to not know.

Argument: Education, media, philosophy, and everyday discourse normalize white perspectives as universal, making racial hierarchy appear either natural or nonexistent. Nonwhite thinkers experience “double consciousness”: seeing themselves through their own eyes and through the racist gaze.

Critique: This dimension directly attacks the Enlightenment belief in neutral Reason. It also explains why traditional political philosophy is largely silent on race: the discipline itself is formed inside the racial contract.

  

### **6. Racial Contract and Western Political Thought**
#### **6.1 FirstWave of Contractualism (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau)**

Viewpoint: The “golden age” of social contract theory coincides with colonial expansion; canonical theorists embed racial assumptions in their accounts of the state of nature and property. [S9 PPT]

Evidence – Hobbes: S9 引语：“It may be thought there was never such a time as this… and I believe it was never generally so, over all the world; but there are many places, where they live so now… the savage people in many places of America… have no government at all; and live at this day in that brutish manner.” [S9 PPT]

Assumption: For Hobbes, the state of nature is partly illustrated by contemporary non-European peoples; they exemplify the pre-political “war of all against all.”

Argument: This links the thought experiment directly to a racialized geography: Europeans = civil society; Indigenous Americans = state of nature.

Evidence – Locke: Locke’s theory of property and acquisition legitimizes dispossession of Native populations: uncultivated land can be appropriated by those who “improve” it. [S9 PPT][Mills book]

Assumption: Only European forms of agriculture and private property count as “improvement.” Indigenous uses of land are erased.

Argument: Social contract + property theory = ideological justification of conquest and settlement.

Evidence – Rousseau: Even Rousseau’s “noble savage” imagines non-Europeans as natural, pre-political beings, still grounding civilization in a white norm. [S9 PPT]

Critique: Mills’ reading shows the first wave of contractualism is not race-neutral; it offers **philosophical cover for colonialism**.

  ### **6.2 Second Wave of Contractualism (Rawls, Nozick)**

Viewpoint: 20th-century contract theories continue the silence: race and colonial history largely disappear from accounts of justice. [S9 PPT]

Evidence: Rawls’s _A Theory of Justice_ uses an abstract original position and barely mentions slavery or segregation; Nozick develops a theory of property with no serious engagement with the history of conquest and expropriation. [S9 PPT][Mills book]

Assumptions: Ideal theory brackets away historical injustice; justice is defined as fairness among already-present citizens, ignoring how they came to possess their holdings.

Argument: This constitutes another layer of the epistemology of ignorance: moral philosophy’s “silence” on white supremacy.

Critique: Mills calls for a **“nonideal” contract theory** that starts from racial injustice as basic fact, not as anomaly.

### **7. Politics of Race: Strategies and Positions**
#### **7.1 Viewpoint**

S9 总结了三种政治立场来应对种族契约：[S9 PPT]
1. Reformist
2. Abolitionist
3. Median/structural-relational position
    
#### **7.2 Reformist Position**
- Focus on individual behaviors and everyday practices.
- Race is reproduced in micro-interactions (microaggressions, prejudice).
    Assumption: Structural change follows from changing individual attitudes.
    Critique: Risks psychologizing race; may underplay structural economic and institutional dimensions of the racial contract. [S9 PPT]
    
### **7.3 Abolitionist Position**
- Aims at abolishing racial social structures altogether; often inspired by Marxist thought.
- Sometimes described as “symbolic suicide of white people” (whites must renounce their racial identity as a privileged position). [S9 PPT]
    Assumption: Racial categories are so bound up with domination that they must be destroyed, not reformed.
    Critique: Can appear utopian or impractical; also raises questions about political agency in the present.
    

#### **7.4 Median Position (Structure and Individual)**
- Sees race as both **structural and individual**.
- Uses concepts like **habitus** (Bourdieu) to connect deep structures with embodied dispositions. [S9 PPT]
    Assumption: Structures shape but do not fully determine individual practice; individuals reproduce or contest the racial contract through everyday action.
    Argument: This position fits well with Mills’ emphasis on both material institutions and epistemic habits.
    Critique: Hard to operationalize; abstraction of “structure” can make concrete political strategy unclear, but conceptually it captures Mills’ multi-level conception of the racial contract.
  
## **8. Overall Critical Synthesis (for Essay Use)**

Viewpoint: Mills transforms social contract theory from an ideal, race-neutral narrative into a **critical diagnostic of racial domination**. The racial contract is the hidden meta-contract behind the social contract. [Mills book][S9 PPT

Integrated Argument
1. Historically, the so-called age of social contract is simultaneously the age of colonial expansion and slavery; classical contractarians encode nonwhites as state-of-nature peoples, outside the realm of full personhood.
2. Politically, the racial contract creates a racial polity where whites are persons and nonwhites are subpersons; rights, protections, and citizenship are racially allocated.
3. Morally, universalism is restricted: moral norms apply fully to whites and only partially to nonwhites, justifying violence and expropriation.
4. Epistemically, an “epistemology of ignorance” allows whites to misperceive or ignore racial domination; philosophy participates in this ignorance by its silence.
5. In contemporary “post–civil rights” or “post-colonial” contexts, the racial contract persists informally as structural inequality, spatial segregation, and global economic hierarchy.
    Critique/Implication: To take social contract theory seriously today, we must **rebuild it on nonideal grounds**, beginning from the reality of race and colonial history. Any theory of justice that abstracts away from the racial contract reproduces the epistemology of ignorance.
    
    中文总括：传统社会契约把“平等、理性、公民”当作出发点；Mills 与 S9 告诉我们，真正的起点是“白人至上”的种族契约。理解社会契约=必须看见它的种族前提，否则自由主义政治哲学就会变成维护既有秩序的意识形态工具。

## Hanna Pitkin 'Standing For'
**critique of descriptive/mirror representation** 
• an over‐emphasis on who is present in the legislative assemblies diverts attention from more urgent questions of<u> what the representatives actually do. </u>
• ‘Think of the legislature as a pictorial representation or a representative sample of the nation, and you will almost certainly concentrate on its composition rather than its activities.’ 
• it is the activities rather than the characteristics that matter, and what happens after the action rather than before it that counts. 
• Representing ‘means acting in the interests of the represented, in a manner responsive to them’ - **this representation takes on a symbolic form rather than a descriptive one**

### Symbolic VS Descriptive 
• To advance her critique of decriptive representation, Pitkin draws a distinction between the symbolic and the descriptive 
• "A symbol is not a source of information about what it represents; it does not allege anything about what it represents. (…) Rather than a source of information, the symbol seems to be the recipient or object of feelings, expressions of feeling, or actions intended for what it represents." (99) 
• We make symbols not because they literally describe anything but because it will come to stand for what we believe or want it to stand for "symbols stand for whatever they are currently taken or defined to stand for." 
• The symbol used to represent something is arbitrary – we have to pay attention to the what the symbol stands for (instead of what it actually looks like)

### Critique
- Standing for the interests of the represented, especially those who are marginalized or oppressed, **might require some understanding of the lived experience of marginalization** 
- **A purely interest-based model of representation is insufficient because interests are shaped by social identity** (a working class person is better equipped to represent the interests of working class people)
- Descriptive representation is miscontrued in Pitkin's account as merely pertaining to a kind of surface-level view of mirroring when in fact social identity pertains also **to set of experiences that are useful for informing political judgment**
- **For Anne Phillips, political institutions lacking women or minorities cannot credibly claim to represent them.**

## Anne Phillips 'The Politics of Presence

- <u>Phillips advances a case for a politics of presence which advocates for a descriptive form of representation for the simple reason that those who belong to a particular social identity are better equipped to represent the interests of that particular group </u>
- Presence = descriptive representation with democratic purpose: Certain groups must be physically present in decision-making bodies because: 
	- (1) Exclusion distorts which interests are heard; 
	- (2) Lived experience influences agendasetting and issue framing; 
	- (3) Representative claims lack legitimacy when systematically non-inclusive.
	  
- presence enhances the quality of substantive representation.

### Critique
- It is **possible for representatives to act against the interests of their group** even if they share the same social identity! 
- Who is an authentic 'representer' of a particular group (in the case of certain social ethnic, racial, gendered social identities this could lead to some form of **essentialism** 
	- 本质主义：**本质主义 = 认为一个群体有一个统一、不变、内在的“本质”。**
		- “所有女性都有一样的政治关切”
		- “所有黑人都支持同样的政策”
		- “所有移民都想要同样的权利”
		 **它假装一个群体内部没有差异**
		 **它忽视了个体经历的多样性**
		 **它让某些人假装自己最‘正统’、最‘真实’**
- Who counts? Who defines the group identity? Shouldn't group interests matter more than identity
