# World History Final Exam Review

# PART ONE: THEMATIC INDEX OF HISTORICAL EVENTS

This section organizes course material under three thematic headings, with sub-categories under each. Every entry provides time, place, key actors, causes, process, outcome, and any scholarly perspectives from the course.

---

## THEME A: VIOLENCE AND CONFLICT

### A1. Interstate and Imperial War

**World War I (1914–1918)**

Time and place: 1914 to 1918, fought across Europe, the Middle East, Africa, East Asia and the Pacific.

Key actors: The Allied/Entente Powers (France, Britain, Russia, later the United States, Italy, Japan, China) against the Central Powers (Germany, Austria-Hungary, the Ottoman Empire, Bulgaria). Gavrilo Princip, a Bosnian Serb nationalist, assassinated Archduke Franz Ferdinand on June 28, 1914, which triggered the chain of declarations.

Causes: Overlapping alliance systems turned a regional crisis into a continental war within days. Underlying factors included naval arms races between Britain and Germany, intensifying nationalism (particularly French-German rivalry over Alsace-Lorraine), imperial competition, and what historian Christopher Clark calls a series of "calculated steps" rather than a single miscalculation.

Process: The war became "total war" for the first time in modern history, meaning it mobilized entire societies. Around 70 million soldiers served; states controlled railways, munitions factories, and food distribution. Women entered industrial labor at scale (by August 1916, 334,000 British women worked in munitions factories). Colonial subjects were drawn in: 1.4 million Indians, 600,000 soldiers and 200,000 workers from French colonies, and 200,000 Chinese laborers who dug trenches and buried dead on the Western Front. The war also produced the first attempted elimination of an entire people, the Armenian genocide in which approximately 1.5 million perished.

Outcome: 8.5 million soldiers killed and 21 million wounded. The war ended the Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, Russian, and German empires. The Treaty of Versailles (signed June 28, 1919) imposed reparations on Germany and redrew borders across Europe and the Middle East. The League of Nations was created in 1920.

Scholar perspectives: Jay Winter (historian) argues that WWI is "the foundational disaster of the twentieth century" because without it "there would be no Hitler, no Lenin, no Auschwitz." Jan Patocka (philosopher) described it as the moment when the world became "a laboratory for releasing reserves of energy accumulated over billions of years." Historian Eric Weitz frames the postwar settlement as a shift from the "Vienna system" (dynastic sovereignty over multi-ethnic populations) to the "Paris system" (population politics and national homogeneity).

Extendable to: the concept of total war, mobilization of women and colonized peoples, the origin of modern refugee crises, and the precedent for WWII.

---

**World War II: European and Pacific Theaters (1937/1939–1945)**

Time and place: Full-scale war in Asia began with the Marco Polo Bridge Incident in July 1937; in Europe with Germany's invasion of Poland in September 1939. The war ended in Europe on May 8, 1945 and in the Pacific on August 15, 1945.

Key actors: Allied Powers (the UK, France, the USSR, the US, China) against the Axis (Germany, Italy, Japan). Key leaders included Roosevelt, Churchill, Stalin, De Gaulle, Hitler, Mussolini, Hirohito, Chiang Kai-shek, and Mao Zedong.

Causes: In Europe, the unresolved legacies of WWI combined with the Great Depression, the rise of fascist and Nazi ideologies, and the failure of appeasement (the 1938 Munich agreement). In Asia, Japanese imperial expansion began with the seizure of Manchuria in 1931 and escalated into full-scale invasion of China in 1937.

Process: The war featured unprecedented destruction and ideological violence. The Eastern Front accounted for three quarters of all combat casualties. Nazism combined charismatic leadership with bureaucratic rationalization in what Ian Kershaw calls "cumulative radicalization" and continent-wide ethnic cleansing. The January 1942 Wannsee Conference formalized the Holocaust. In Asia, the Nanjing Massacre (1937), the use of "comfort women," and sustained bombing of Chongqing for nearly seven years illustrated the scale of Japanese wartime violence. Allied bombing also escalated: firebombing destroyed large portions of German and Japanese cities, and the first Tokyo raid in March 1945 killed an estimated 100,000. The US dropped atomic bombs on Hiroshima (August 6) and Nagasaki (August 9).

Outcome: Approximately 70 to 85 million dead worldwide. In China alone, perhaps 20 million died over eight years of fighting, with 80 million displaced. The war ended European colonial prestige and set conditions for decolonization. The United Nations was created in 1945. The US and USSR emerged as rival superpowers, launching the Cold War.

Scholar perspective: Kershaw describes Nazism as "the institutionalization of resentment," in which the state set people against each other by exploiting "every conceivable cleavage in society." Hobsbawm's framework of the "age of extremes" connects the ideological violence of WWII to the earlier crisis of liberal civilization.

---

**The Korean War (1950–1953)**

Time and place: The Korean peninsula, from June 1950 to July 1953.

Key actors: North Korea (under Kim Il-sung, supported by China and the USSR) and South Korea (supported by a US-led UN coalition under General Douglas MacArthur).

Causes: The partition of Korea along the 38th parallel after WWII created two rival states. Cold War competition between the US and USSR turned the peninsula into a proxy conflict.

Process: The US dropped more bombs on Korea than it had during the entire Pacific Theater of WWII. The US spent $3 billion in Japan for supplies, which contributed to Japan's postwar economic recovery.

Outcome: The war ended in an armistice, not a peace treaty, with the border roughly restored to the 38th parallel. Millions of Korean civilians died. The conflict consolidated Cold War divisions in East Asia.

---

**The Vietnam War (1954–1975)**

Time and place: Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia, from the mid-1950s through 1975.

Key actors: North Vietnam (Ho Chi Minh, supported by China and the USSR), South Vietnam (Ngo Dinh Diem, later various military governments, backed by the US), and eventually the broader anti-war movement in the US. Journalist I.F. Stone was a prominent critic. Defense Secretary Robert McNamara later commissioned "The Pentagon Papers."

Causes: After French defeat at Dien Bien Phu (1954) and the Geneva partition, the US increasingly committed resources to prevent communist expansion in Southeast Asia. By 1963, the US was spending $400 million annually with 12,000 military advisors, yet 63% of Americans paid "little or no attention."

Process: The 1964 Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (with only two dissenting votes) authorized President Johnson to escalate. The war became a major ground conflict by 1965. I.F. Stone captured the logic of destruction by noting that making 10,000 peasants homeless to get at 100 guerrillas was "guaranteed to create 10 new rebels for every one." Nixon secretly expanded bombing to Cambodia from March 1969. The US withdrew forces in 1973.

Outcome: Saigon fell in April 1975. The war killed an estimated 2 to 3 million Vietnamese and approximately 58,000 Americans. It deeply divided US society and contributed to disillusionment with Cold War interventionism.

Extendable to: media and public opinion, counterinsurgency doctrine (Roger Trinquier's "pacification"), the Khmer Rouge genocide (1975–1979, killing roughly 25% of Cambodia's 7 million people), and the turn toward human rights activism in the late 1970s.

---

**The Algerian War (1954–1962)**

Time and place: Algeria and metropolitan France, 1954 to 1962.

Key actors: The Front de Liberation Nationale (FLN), French military (including Lt Col Roger Trinquier), French politicians (Mendes-France, Guy Mollet, De Gaulle), and Algerian civilians.

Causes: Over a century of French colonial rule, combined with the post-WWII wave of anti-colonial movements across the developing world.

Process: The FLN launched attacks on November 1, 1954. France responded with increasing force: the State of Emergency Law (1955), Special Powers Law (1956), and the Battle of Algiers (September 1956 to September 1957) involved systematic "pacification" tactics. Trinquier's counterinsurgency methods were studied alongside those used in Malaya and Kenya by the US, UK, and France. The contradictions of colonial modernization were visible: France invested in infrastructure, education, and land reform but delivered all of it through 700 civil-military units.

Outcome: The Evian Accords (1962) ended the war. An estimated 300,000 Algerians were killed, a quarter of the population was placed in camps, and one million were dispersed around the Mediterranean. The war destroyed the French Fourth Republic (May 1958) and brought De Gaulle to power with a strengthened presidency.

Scholar perspective: Frantz Fanon analyzed the corruptive psychology of colonial violence; Jean-Paul Sartre's preface to Fanon's _The Wretched of the Earth_ (1961) offered a _tiers-mondiste_ rationalization of anti-colonial violence.

---

**Yugoslavia and Rwanda (1990s)**

Time and place: Yugoslavia (1991–1995) and Rwanda (1994).

Key actors: In Yugoslavia, Serbian president Slobodan Milosevic and Croatian president Franjo Tudjman. In Rwanda, the Hutu-led government and Paul Kagame's Tutsi forces.

Causes: The dissolution of Yugoslavia was driven by competing ethnic nationalisms released after the end of the Cold War. In Rwanda, the 1990 Tutsi invasion from Uganda and longstanding Hutu-Tutsi tensions escalated after President Habyarimana's plane was shot down in 1994.

Process: The Yugoslav wars (1991–1995) included the murder of approximately 200,000 Bosnian Muslims through ethnic cleansing. The Dayton Accords (1995) ended the fighting. In Rwanda, radio-driven mass killing of Tutsis and sympathetic Hutus was followed by Kagame's counter-offensive. Less well known, Kagame later pursued and killed an estimated 200,000 Hutu refugees in the Congo (1996).

Outcome: Both conflicts exposed the failure of the international community to prevent genocide, leading to the Responsibility to Protect (R2P) doctrine in 2001.

---

**The Iraq War and the "War on Terror" (2001–2011)**

Time and place: Primarily Afghanistan (from 2001) and Iraq (2003–2011), with global implications.

Key actors: The Bush administration (Vice President Dick Cheney, Defense Secretary Rumsfeld, Deputy Paul Wolfowitz), Colin Powell (UN presentation), Saddam Hussein.

Causes: The September 11, 2001 attacks provided the immediate catalyst. The neoconservative faction within the Bush administration pushed for regime change in Iraq, claiming weapons of mass destruction.

Process: The Patriot Act expanded the surveillance state. The US opened Guantanamo Bay detention facility. The Iraq war began in March 2003. The Abu Ghraib scandal (April 2004) revealed systematic torture. The Islamic State later emerged from the power vacuum (2013–2017).

Outcome: The war destroyed the post-Cold War consensus around US liberal hegemony and deepened instability across the Middle East.

Scholar context: Samuel Huntington's "clash of civilizations" thesis (1993) and Robert Kaplan's "the coming anarchy" (1994) represented darker US elite visions of the post-Cold War order that anticipated this trajectory.

### A2. Civil War, Revolution, and Internal Conflict

**The Russian Revolution (1917)**

Time and place: Russia, February and October 1917.

Key actors: The Bolsheviks (Lenin), the Provisional Government, workers, soldiers, and women who sparked the February Revolution.

Causes: Short-term causes included army demoralization, food shortages, and a strike movement against the war, triggered on March 8 (International Women's Day). Long-term causes included eroding autocracy, spreading class-consciousness, expanding education, and the international socialist movement.

Process: The February Revolution overthrew the Tsar. The Bolsheviks seized power on November 7, 1917 (Military Revolutionary Committee, capture of Winter Palace), establishing a "dictatorship of the proletariat." The Treaty of Brest-Litovsk (March 1918) ended Russia's role in WWI. A brutal Civil War (1918–1921) and famine (1921–1922) followed.

Outcome: Creation of the Soviet Union (Congress of Soviets, 1922) and the founding of the Comintern (March 1919), which aimed to spread communist revolution internationally.

Lenin's critique: Lenin extended Marxist analysis to imperialism and to democracies "where people are too weary to participate in public life." On Taylorism, Lenin noted that while workers' productivity quadrupled under the system, their pay only increased by half "and only for a short period at that."

---

**The Chinese Revolution and Civil War (1927–1949)**

Time and place: China, from the Nationalist purge of the CCP in April 1927 through the founding of the PRC on October 1, 1949.

Key actors: Chiang Kai-shek (Nationalist/GMD), Mao Zedong (CCP), Sun Yat-sen (died 1925), Lin Biao (CCP commander).

Process: After the Nationalist purge of 1927, the CCP was reduced to outlaws in isolated base areas. The Nationalists ruled from Nanjing (1928–1937) while the CCP retreated to Yan'an. During WWII, the Nationalist army bore the brunt of fighting against Japan but was weakened: cities ruined (Nanjing three-quarters destroyed, Shanghai half), hyperinflation (a bag of rice costing $12 in 1938 cost $63 million by war's end), and 1,716 strikes in Shanghai alone in 1946. The CCP grew from 40,000 to 1 million members and from 92,000 to 900,000 troops between 1937 and 1945.

CCP strategies: Socially, the CCP used "speak bitterness sessions" that locked in party support by giving voice and agency to poor peasants while creating fear of reprisal. Militarily, with Soviet weapons, Lin Biao captured 250,000 of the best Nationalist troops in a single 1948 campaign.

Outcome: Mao proclaimed the PRC on October 1, 1949, while Chiang Kai-shek fled to Taiwan. The CCP had 3 million party members governing 500 million people.

---

**The Chinese Cultural Revolution (1966–1976, most intense 1966–1970)**

Time and place: China, beginning spring 1966.

Key actors: Mao Zedong, Lin Biao, Chen Boda (Mao's aide who took over _People's Daily_ in May 1966).

Causes: The Socialist Education Movement (1963–1966) laid cultural groundwork. Mao's faction launched the revolution against "capitalist roaders" within the party bureaucracy. References to "Mao Zedong Thought" in _People's Daily_ increased from fewer than 100 in 1963 to 5,000 per year by the late 1960s.

Process: Three overlapping stages characterized the most intense phase. First, mid-to-late 1966 saw student rebellion and the formation of mass organizations targeting local governments. Second, summer 1967 saw peak armed battles between insurgent factions. Third, spring 1968 brought PLA intervention to disband mass organizations and establish "revolutionary committees" in 90% of localities by early 1969, followed by the "Cleansing of Class Ranks Campaign."

Outcome: The revolution devastated Chinese institutions, destroyed cultural heritage, and caused an unknown number of deaths. It is debated whether it represented rural justice for the Great Leap Forward, the personal cult of Mao, or a fusion of wartime socialism with Cold War politics.

Extendable to: the global spread of Maoism (Louis Althusser, Godard, Sartre in France; Editions Seuil published the _Quotations_ in 1967; Maoist movements appeared in student radicalism worldwide in 1968).

---

**The Iranian Revolution (1979)**

Time and place: Iran, 1978–1979.

Key actors: Ruhollah Khomeini (exiled cleric), the Shah, the middle-class Freedom Movement, and industrial workers.

Causes: The Freedom Movement of the 1960s was ideologically mixed (Marxist, revolutionary, Islamist, nativist). Meanwhile, Khomeini used cassette tape lectures from Paris to inspire Islamist opposition among the working classes, exploiting the Shia tradition of identification with the oppressed. Policies against inflation from booming oil prices exacerbated unemployment and workers' grievances.

Process: A cycle of protests, killings, and funerals/memorials escalated through 1978. Tehran's "Black Friday" (September 8, 1978) saw gunships and tanks used against protestors. Mass strikes paralyzed the country. The Shah fled in January 1979; Khomeini returned from Paris in February.

Outcome: A national referendum created a dual power structure: a secular elected government overseen by a clerical establishment led by Khomeini. Revolutionary Guards formed as a parallel power center. From November 1979 to January 1981, US embassy staff were held hostage. Iraq invaded in 1980. Women's rights were curtailed (mandatory hijab, loss of many legal protections) though women retained the right to vote and became politically mobilized. Iran supported Hezbollah's founding in 1982.

---

**The Tiananmen Movement and Massacre (1989)**

Time and place: Beijing and cities across China, April to June 1989.

Key actors: Hu Yaobang (whose death triggered protests), students, workers and cadres, Zhao Ziyang (reformist), Li Peng (hardliner), Deng Xiaoping.

Process: The death of Hu Yaobang on April 15, 1989 (a symbol of liberal politics, removed from the line of succession in 1987 for lenience on student protestors) triggered mass marches. May saw spontaneous unofficial press freedoms. A power struggle between Zhao Ziyang and Li Peng played out publicly. Martial law was declared on May 20. On June 4, thousands were killed.

Outcome: The massacre ended the possibility of political liberalization in China, while economic reform continued. On the same day (June 4, 1989), Communists lost big in Polish elections, highlighting the divergent paths of communist states.

---

**The Arab Spring (2011)**

Time and place: Tunisia, Egypt, Libya, Syria, and other countries across North Africa and the Middle East.

Key actors: Mohamed Bouazizi (Tunisian fruitseller whose suicide sparked the movement), Presidents Ben Ali (Tunisia), Mubarak (Egypt), Gaddafi (Libya), Assad (Syria).

Process: Tunisian protests in December 2010 led Ben Ali to flee in January 2011. Egypt's Tahrir Square protests forced Mubarak's resignation on February 11, 2011, but were followed by the election of Islamist Mohamed Morsi (June 2012) and General el-Sisi's coup (June 2013). NATO bombing helped topple Gaddafi in Libya by August 2011. In Syria, protests starting March 2011 escalated into civil war lasting until Assad's fall in 2024, displacing 5 million Syrians.

Outcome: Only Tunisia achieved a democratic transition. The Syrian conflict created Europe's largest migration crisis since WWII.

### A3. Genocide and Mass Violence

**The Armenian Genocide (1915)**

Time and place: Ottoman Empire, during WWI.

Process: The first attempted elimination of an entire people in the 20th century. Approximately 1.5 million Armenians perished. The genocide occurred within the broader context of WWI's total mobilization and the displacement/exchange of populations that characterized the late Ottoman period.

---

**The Holocaust (1933–1945)**

Time and place: Germany and occupied Europe.

Process: From the 1933 Nazi seizure of power, through _Kristallnacht_ (November 1938), to the systematic genocide formalized at the Wannsee Conference (January 1942). Six million Jews murdered along with millions of Roma, Slavs, disabled people, and others.

Scholar perspective: Kershaw identifies Nazism's "cumulative radicalization" and its combination of Max Weber's charismatic power with bureaucratic rationalization. The Nazi state had "pseudo-religious apocalyptic qualities: salvation vs. extermination."

---

**The Great Leap Forward Famine (1958–1962)**

Time and place: China, especially rural areas.

Key actors: Mao Zedong, General Peng Dehuai (critic, purged after the Lushan Conference in July 1959).

Causes: Mao promoted "continuing revolution" and surpassing Britain in iron and steel production in 15 years. By end of 1958, 740,000 cooperatives were merged into 26,000 communes, with communal kitchens and work brigades.

Process: False prosperity reigned: the 1958 harvest was reported at 375 million tons (around 800 lbs per rural person per year), when the actual crop was only 442 lbs and soon dropped to 338 lbs. Exhaustion from round-the-clock shifts, waste from "backyard furnaces," and ideological distortion of science (close planting, "socialist seeds") compounded the crisis. Realities were concealed by fixed prices, communal kitchens, party-run media, cadres competing to report high yields, and restricted movement.

Outcome: Between 30 and 45 million people perished. Peng Dehuai warned that "putting politics in command is no substitute for economic principles," but was purged. The question historians debate is whether this was an old-style famine or "modernity gone mad."

---

**The Cambodian Genocide (1975–1979)**

Time and place: Cambodia under the Khmer Rouge.

Key actors: Pol Pot (Paris-educated leader of the Communist Party of Kampuchea).

Causes: US bombing of Cambodia (from March 1969, with up to 1,800 tons of bombs per day from B-52s) and the 1970 Lon Nol coup destabilized the country. The Khmer Rouge took Phnom Penh in April 1975.

Outcome: Roughly 25% of Cambodia's 7 million people perished. Vietnam invaded and removed the Khmer Rouge in 1979. Pol Pot lived free until 1998.

---

### A4. Protest, Popular Movements, and Social Conflict

**Global 1968**

Key actors and places: Student and worker movements across France (May 1968), Italy (Valle Giulia, March 1968), the US (civil rights, anti-war), and China (Cultural Revolution). French Maoism attracted intellectuals like Althusser, Godard, and Sartre.

Explanation: The question of how to explain 1960s youth movements and radicalism has both country-specific answers (in China, the figure of Mao and "war communism") and global answers (postwar demography, a new stage of capitalism).

Scholar perspective: Thomas Frank (_The Conquest of Cool_, 1997) argues that "the counterculture may be more accurately understood as a stage in the development of the values of the American middle class, a colorful installment in the twentieth century drama of consumer subjectivity." Frank contends that "hip consumerism" replaced class-based consumption with personality-based consumption.

---

**1980s People's Movements and the Fall of Communism**

Key cases: Korea democratized through student movements (1987 elections after the 1979 Kwangju massacre). The Philippines' "People Power" ousted Marcos in 1986. Chile's 1988 plebiscite denied Pinochet a new term. Poland's Solidarity movement (Lech Walesa) won a Nobel Peace Prize in 1983 and won elections on June 4, 1989. Hungary's "revolution" of 1988–1989 opened borders. The Berlin Wall fell in November 1989. The USSR dissolved by 1992 after Gorbachev's glasnost/perestroika and the failed August 1991 coup.

Extendable to: Francis Fukuyama's "end of history" thesis (1992) versus the contrasting realities of Yugoslavia and Rwanda.

---

**The 2008 Financial Crisis and Its Political Fallout**

Time and place: Global, from September 2008.

Causes: The immediate cause was the subprime mortgage market and toxic derivatives. Underlying causes included financial system acceleration through computing and fiber optics, deregulation of capital flows since the 1980s, and the loss of "circuit breakers" to prevent the spread of collapse. By 2007, the total notional value of derivatives was ten times global GDP.

Process: Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy in September 2008. French, German, UK, and Dutch banks were heavily exposed. Henry Paulson (ex-Goldman Sachs CEO) led quantitative easing. Total support to the Western financial sector amounted to 50.4% of world GDP. Germany's first bailout was for EUR 406 billion.

Political fallout: The Greek debt crisis saw GDP fall 27% over six straight years of austerity (2010–2016). The 2015 Syriza government won a 61.6% referendum "No" to a third bailout, but the prime minister folded to outside pressures.

Scholar: Cedric Durand (_Fictitious Capital_) analyzes the crisis through Marx's framework of private credit, public bonds, shadow banking, and financial innovation.

---

**Occupy, the Arab Spring, and Contemporary Protest (2011–2019)**

Cases: Occupy Wall Street (September 2011, Zuccotti Park, "We are the 99%," inspired by anthropologist David Graeber, following the 2010 Citizens United Supreme Court decision). The Hong Kong Umbrella Movement (2014 and 2019–2020, with Joshua Wong, Nathan Law, and Alex Chow). Extinction Rebellion (founded 2018, blocked five London bridges, occupied Parliament Square).

### A5. Gender Conflict and Women's Mobilization

**Women in WWI:** Shift from domestic service to wartime industries. In the UK, 334,000 women worked in munitions factories by August 1916. Most women aged 30 or older received the vote in March 1918, but by 1921 the percentage of women in the workforce returned to 1910 levels. Margaret Sanger launched a global campaign for birth control, though it intersected with eugenics and state demands on childbearers.

**Women's education:** Japan Women's University opened in 1901; Shreemati Nathibai Damodar Thackersey Women's University in Mumbai in 1916; first women at Peking University in 1920; the "Seven Sisters" consortium in the US in 1926.

**Japan's gender politics in the 1930s:** The League to Abolish Licensed Prostitution turned into the National Purification Federation focused on "purification of blood" (eugenics) in 1935. The 1937 Mother and Child Protection Act passed the same year Kato Shidzue was arrested for promoting birth control.

**Iran:** After the 1979 revolution, hijab became mandatory and women lost many legal protections, yet they retained the right to vote and became politically mobilized.

## THEME B: IDEOLOGY

### B1. Nationalism and Self-Determination

**The shift from empire to nation-state** is one of the defining transformations of the 20th century. At the start of the century, multi-ethnic empires (Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, British, French) dominated world politics. Benedict Anderson's concept of "imagined communities" (1983) explains how technologies of colonial administration (mapping, ethnology, demography) helped create national consciousness.

Key cases: Serbian nationalism triggering WWI. Woodrow Wilson's doctrine of self-determination at the Paris Peace Conference. The "21 demands" by Japan on China (1915) and the May Fourth Movement (1919). Indian independence movements from the 1885 Indian National Congress through Gandhian civil disobedience. The Bandung Conference (1955), where Zhou Enlai and Sukarno articulated Afro-Asian solidarity against colonialism.

Anthony Reid (2010) describes decolonization as requiring "imperial alchemy": the base metal of empire transmitted into "the gold of nationhood."

---

### B2. Communism and Socialism

**Bolshevism and Leninism:** The 1917 Revolution created the world's first communist state. Lenin's critique combined anti-imperialism with a theory of how capitalism exploited workers under Taylorism while offering only temporary, partial gains. The Comintern (1919) spread revolutionary ideology globally.

**Maoism:** Mao combined Marxism-Leninism with mass mobilization and "continuous revolution." His September 1949 speech declared: "Since they learned Marxism-Leninism, the Chinese people have ceased to be passive in spirit and gained the initiative." The PRC's First Five-Year Plan (1953–1957) used Soviet-model industrialization, established the danwei (work unit) system and the "iron rice bowl." The hukou household registration system (1958) codified rural-urban division. Maoism spread internationally through foreign-language translations and inspired movements from France to Africa.

**The global spread of Marxism in Africa:** Communist ideas circulated in Africa from the early 20th century. Postwar strikes against resource extraction by imperial powers provided organizing grounds. Anti-communism was used to rationalize suppressing African nationalist movements (as in South Africa). The Cold War competition between the US, USSR, and China created "client states" across the continent.

---

### B3. Fascism and Nazism

**Italian Fascism:** Mussolini abandoned socialism for a new ideology that was militaristic, born of WWI, anti-Marxist (rejecting class struggle) and anti-liberal (rejecting individualism). It emphasized the primacy of national community, authoritarian charismatic leadership, and the belief that violence expressed personal and national vitality. The _squadristi_ fought socialist unionists (1919–1921) before the October 1922 March on Rome.

The social conditions enabling fascism's rise included PSI's policy of neutrality during WWI, disappointment with parliamentary democracy after the war, widespread unemployment, a divided Italian government, the rise of communism among industrial workers, and middle-class fear of a Russian-style revolution. Jason Stanley (_How Fascism Works_, 2018) notes that fascist propaganda aimed at spreading general suspicion and destroying mutual trust among citizens.

Before 1938, Mussolini had no race laws. The 1938 Manifesto of Race declared Italy populated by people of Aryan origins and excluded Italian Jews.

Seminar discussion also noted that fascism was an "outcome of the amalgamation of two main intellectual trends: anti-materialist revision of Marxism and organic nationalism," with France as the intellectual birthplace of fascist ideology even though it never conquered the political sphere there.

**German Nazism:** Born of post-war crisis (unemployment, inflation, Versailles), adding "redemptive anti-Semitism" and racial struggle to Italian fascist themes. The _Volksgemeinschaft_ (People's Community) promised an end to class struggle, social mobility based on performance, and preparation for war through community. Electoral support grew from 2.6% in 1928 to 37.4% by June 1932. Kershaw describes Nazi rule as combining charismatic power with bureaucratic rationalization and having "pseudo-religious apocalyptic qualities."

**Japanese militarism:** The Showa period saw ultranationalist civilian groups and small businesses form an anti-capitalist, anti-party movement. Pan-Asianism (inspired by eclectic writer Kita Ikki) combined socialism with imperialism under the slogan "Asia for Asians." The February 26, 1936 coup led to military domination over civilian governance. Japan's "Co-Prosperity Sphere" instrumentalized anti-imperialism to legitimize Japanese imperial hierarchy.

Connection between fascism and imperialism (seminar essay topic): Fascist regimes radicalized imperial practices by breaking the liberal world order (Japan's invasion of Manchuria 1931, Italy's invasion of Ethiopia 1935, collapse of the League of Nations). Concentration camps were identified as "a colonial technology" with entanglements between fascist and liberal imperial practices.

---

### B4. Liberalism, Free Markets, and the "Washington Consensus"

**The New Deal:** FDR's response to the Great Depression launched the modern welfare state. The Social Security Act (1935) was not a welfare system but an income guarantee that all contributed to and received. Redlining (Federal Housing Administration maps) embedded structural racial segregation into New Deal housing policy.

**Bretton Woods (1944):** Created the World Bank (first loan to France, 1947), the IMF with "structural adjustments," and pegged member currencies to the US dollar. Daniel Immerwahr (_How to Hide an Empire_, 2019) examines US client states. The Ford and Rockefeller Foundations became instruments of US soft power.

**Neoliberalism:** The 1980s rightward turn under Thatcher and Reagan deregulated capital flows and unleashed financialization. The repeal of the Glass-Steagall Act (1999) and Alan Greenspan's Fed (1987–2006) set conditions for the 2008 crisis.

**Alice Amsden on the "developmental state":** Amsden (_Third World Industrialisation_, 1990) challenged orthodox free-market explanations of East Asian growth. She argued that faster growth in Japan, Korea, and Taiwan was "attributable less to freer markets than to the institutions that have allowed subsidies to be allocated more effectively than elsewhere," and that the state was "transformed to investor in the course of economic development."

---

### B5. Third Worldism, Anti-Colonialism, and Human Rights

**Third Worldism:** Alfred Sauvy coined _tiers-monde_ in 1952. The 1955 Bandung Conference represented Afro-Asian solidarity. Che Guevara's 1964 UN speech argued that without breaking from capitalist markets and imposing new trade terms, there would be "no sound economic development." Frantz Fanon (_The Wretched of the Earth_, 1961) analyzed colonial psychology. Sartre coined "neocolonialism" in 1964 to describe France's continued economic and cultural relationships with former colonies.

Negritude was a movement developed by francophone intellectuals in the 1930s that aimed at raising Black consciousness across Africa and its diaspora, functioning as a framework of critique and literary theory.

Two phases of decolonization: (1) 1945–1955, mainly Southeast Asia and the Middle East (India 1947, Burma 1948, Indonesia 1949, Vietnam partition 1954); (2) 1955 onward, mainly North Africa and sub-Saharan Africa.

**The turn to human rights:** By the end of the 1970s, Western leftists were disillusioned by the Khmer Rouge genocide and the 1979 Soviet invasion of Afghanistan. Human rights replaced Third Worldism. Carter's 1977 inaugural stated "our commitment to human rights must be absolute." The _New York Times_ featured "human rights" five times more frequently in 1977 than in any prior year (historian Samuel Moyn). Amnesty International received the 1977 Nobel Peace Prize. Helsinki Watch (later Human Rights Watch) was founded in 1978. Radical leftist energies were rechanneled into humanitarian activism, exemplified by Medecins Sans Frontieres (founded 1971, historian Eleanor Davey).

---

### B6. Environmentalism

**Rachel Carson** (_Silent Spring_, 1962) studied pesticide use worldwide from 1958 to 1962. Robert Paine coined "keystone species" in 1969. The first "Earth Day" was April 22, 1970 (after the 1969 Santa Barbara oil spill, UNESCO-sponsored, with the United Auto Workers' support). Nixon signed the Environmental Protection Agency into existence in 1970; the Endangered Species Act passed Congress nearly without dissent in 1973. The Club of Rome's _The Limits of Growth_ (1972) used MIT computer modelling to argue that finite resources would ultimately halt industrial capacity.

Arundhati Roy (_The Cost of Living_, 1999) criticized India's nuclear arms race and the displacement of unknown millions by 3,300 large dams built since 1950. NASA's James Hansen testified to Congress about the "greenhouse effect" in June 1988. The IPCC held its first meeting in November 1988.

The course's seminar explored environmental history as an interdisciplinary approach with three chief areas: the study of material environment history, political and policy-related environmental history, and cultural/intellectual environmental history.

## THEME C: TECHNOLOGY, ECONOMY, AND DEVELOPMENT

### C1. Industrialization and Production Systems

**Taylorism and Fordism:** Taylorism involved management taking control of the work process and reducing workers to simple, standardized, repeated tasks. Fordism added the assembly line as a control principle. Charlie Chaplin's _Modern Times_ dramatized the dehumanizing effects.

**Soviet industrialization:** The USSR's First Five-Year Plan and subsequent plans took Soviet output from 5% to 18% of global manufactured goods between 1929 and 1938.

**Japanese production model:** The _keiretsu_ "enterprise groups" partnered trading companies with banks. Toyota's "just-in-time" supply strategy (Japan's "pull" versus the US "push" system, where GM devoted a quarter of its workers to fixing defects) became a global model. Japan outsourced resource extraction to the Philippines, Malaysia, and Indonesia, insulating itself from environmental consequences.

**Nike and global supply chains:** Nike evolved from distributing Japanese shoes (Onitsuka Tiger/Asics) into an independent brand that made nothing, only designing, marketing, and distributing. By the 2000s, Nike outsourced to 900 factories worldwide. This model of decoupling corporations from workforces left education, training, and pay to other firms and societies.

---

### C2. Agricultural Modernization and the "Green Revolution"

**India's Green Revolution:** Following the 1943 Bengal famine, independent India launched its First Five-Year Plan (1951–1956). The Ford Foundation's Community Development Program (1951) and the Rockefeller Foundation's agro-research pushed US agricultural models onto tropical zones, often ignoring local knowledge. India's Technical Cooperation Program (1952) opened 24 soil testing centers. The "Green Revolution" (a term only coined in 1968 by a USAID official) brought production gains but also fertilizer dependency (debt, soil depletion), irrigation problems (falling water tables, energy costs), and corporate seed dependency.

**China's Great Leap Forward (1958–1962):** Already covered under mass violence above. The fusion of ideological "hyper optimism" over modernity's gains with political repression and concealed realities produced the century's largest famine.

---

### C3. Communications and Media Technology

**Radio:** The first commercial broadcast was in Pittsburgh on November 2, 1920, announcing Warren Harding's election. Radio and the loudspeaker created passive entertainment compared to concerts or the telephone, and became tools of political mobilization (propaganda in fascist and communist states, Khomeini's cassette tapes in 1970s Iran, radio-driven mass killings in Rwanda).

**Television and media:** CNN launched June 1, 1980. The 1985 "Live Aid" concert at Wembley (produced by Bob Geldof) and USA for Africa combined celebrity activism with television globalization. Fox launched in 1989, reshaping US media culture.

**Digital media:** Facebook and social media played a role in the 2011 Arab Spring. The Hong Kong protests (2019) used digital organizing.

---

### C4. Economic Reform and Development Models

**PRC Economic Reforms (1978):** Farm production shifted from command to contractual basis. Rural consumer-oriented Township-Village Enterprises (TVEs) created 130 million jobs. Special Economic Zones (SEZs) from 1980 drew Japanese, Taiwanese, and Korean investment. State-owned enterprises were exposed to competition, and many urban factories were dismantled, especially in the Northeast.

**The 1997 Asian Financial Crisis:** Hit Thailand, Indonesia, and South Korea hardest. Indonesia's Suharto stepped down in 1998. The crisis contributed to the 1999 Seattle anti-WTO protests and the "sweat-free campus" movement in the US.

**Xi Jinping's China (2012–present):** Xi assumed all three top positions in 2012–2013. He issued the "Seven Nos" at universities (2012), silenced the Southern Media Group (2013), announced the Belt and Road Initiative (2013), launched an anti-corruption drive (2014–2015), and oversaw the Uyghur mass re-education camps (from 2017). Presidential term limits were removed in 2018.

---

### C5. Global Commodity Chains

**Rubber:** A natural South American product, rubber was industrialized in Congo and Southeast Asia. By 1920, British Malaya and the Dutch East Indies were the biggest producers. The price crashed to 1/200th of its 1910 level by 1932. Ford Motor Co. bought 2.5 million acres in the Brazilian Amazon for rubber plantations in 1927. Tran Tu Binh's memoir of a French Michelin plantation described it as "a hell on earth."

**Oil:** The 1973 OPEC embargo and price hike responded to US support for Israel (after the 1967 Six-Day War and 1973 Yom Kippur War). The wider context was planetary concern: the Club of Rome's _The Limits of Growth_ (1972) warned of resource exhaustion. Nixon and Kissinger made the first presidential visit to Saudi Arabia in 1974.

---

### C6. Globalization

Globalization is defined (from the seminar) as worldwide connectivity through increased circulation of people, capital, commodities, and information, combined with heightened potential for global-scale risks (climate change, transnational drug trade, terrorism). Zygmunt Bauman (2006) described "negative globalization" as the selective globalization of trade and capital alongside surveillance, coercion, crime, and terrorism, all "disdaining territorial sovereignty."

Ethnic categories became "a theater for engagement of uncertainty under the special circumstances of globalization": Yugoslav, Kurd, Sikh, Muslim identities were reshaped into political categories used to claim land, rights, and sovereignty.

# PART TWO: SAMPLE QUESTION ANALYSIS

## The 2021 Final Exam Question

The question asked: "The vast majority of countries which were colonies by the end of the 19th century were still colonies in 1945. Then, quite suddenly, most of these colonies obtained independence in the subsequent fifteen years or so. How does one explain this process? What mechanisms propelled these independence movements? And how has the overall relationship between formerly 'Third World' and 'First World' countries changed in the seventy-five years since the end of World War II?"

Five sources were provided: (1) Zhou Enlai's speech at the 1955 Bandung Conference; (2) Sukarno's opening address at the same conference; (3) Che Guevara's 1964 UN speech; (4) Trinh Cong Son's anti-war song (1969); (5) Alice Amsden's argument about state-led development in East Asia (1990).

### Question Breakdown

The question contains three sub-questions: (a) Why did decolonization happen so rapidly after 1945? (b) What mechanisms drove independence movements? (c) How has the Third World/First World relationship evolved since 1945?

The question requires a chronological arc (1945 to present) but also demands thematic analysis of causes and mechanisms. The best approach is a hybrid chronological-thematic outline.

### Model Essay Outline

**Introduction (approx. 150 words)**

Hook: By 1945, the European empires that had controlled most of Asia and Africa were militarily exhausted and morally discredited by two world wars. Within fifteen years, the colonial world map would be almost entirely redrawn.

Define key terms: "decolonization" (the process by which colonized peoples achieved political independence), "Third World" (Alfred Sauvy's _tiers-monde_, coined 1952), "First World" (the Western capitalist bloc).

Briefly present the corpus: five sources spanning the 1955 Bandung Conference to Alice Amsden's 1990 analysis of developmental states, covering political solidarity, economic critiques, and alternative development paths.

Announce the outline: This essay argues that rapid decolonization after 1945 resulted from the convergence of weakened colonial powers, Cold War competition, and organized anti-colonial movements. The post-independence relationship between the former "Third World" and "First World" shifted from political dependency to economic dependency, and was partially challenged by state-led development in East Asia.

**Part I: Why Decolonization Accelerated After 1945**

Sub-part A: WWII exhausted colonial powers and destroyed the myth of European superiority. Japan's rapid conquest of European colonies in Southeast Asia in 1941–1942 proved that Asian peoples could defeat European armies. The war also mobilized colonial subjects (Indian, African, Vietnamese soldiers) who returned with military experience and political demands.

Sub-part B: Cold War competition created incentives for both superpowers to support decolonization. The US pressured European allies to relinquish colonies, while the USSR and China supported liberation movements. Reference Source 1 (Zhou Enlai) and Source 2 (Sukarno): the Bandung Conference reflected this "profound historical change," with both speakers articulating a vision of Afro-Asian solidarity that drew moral authority from colonial suffering.

**Part II: Mechanisms of Independence Movements**

Sub-part A: Organized political movements combined mass mobilization with ideological frameworks. Frantz Fanon's analysis of colonial violence provided intellectual foundations. Anti-colonial violence (Algeria, Vietnam) and non-violent resistance (India under Gandhi) represented two paths. The Algerian War illustrates how colonial modernization and military pacification coexisted, ultimately failing to prevent independence.

Sub-part B: Ideology provided the glue. Reference Source 3 (Che Guevara): Guevara's 1964 speech articulated a structuralist critique, arguing that without new terms of trade, formerly colonized countries would remain under imperialist domination. This Marxist analysis competed with liberal and Third Worldist frameworks. Reference Source 4 (Trinh Cong Son): the Vietnamese song captures the lived experience of layered domination (Chinese, French, civil war), showing how popular culture expressed anti-colonial sentiment.

**Part III: The Evolving Third World/First World Relationship**

Sub-part A: Political independence did not bring economic independence. Neocolonialism (a concept coined by Sartre in 1964) described the continuation of economic and cultural domination through trade structures, conditional aid, and Bretton Woods institutions. Structural adjustment programs imposed by the IMF constrained national economic policy.

Sub-part B: East Asian states challenged this dynamic. Reference Source 5 (Alice Amsden): Amsden argues that East Asian growth was attributable to effective institutional allocation of subsidies, not free markets, and that the state was "transformed to investor." Japan, Korea, and Taiwan demonstrated that former "Third World" countries could industrialize rapidly through state intervention. China's post-1978 reforms and eventual WTO accession (2001) further complicated the Third World/First World binary.

**Conclusion (approx. 100 words)**

Summarize: Decolonization was driven by the convergence of colonial exhaustion, Cold War competition, and organized anti-colonial politics. The Third World/First World relationship evolved from political colonialism to economic dependency, but was partially challenged by developmental states. Open: The rise of China and other emerging economies has further eroded the Third World/First World binary, while new forms of inequality (financial, digital, environmental) have created fresh lines of division.

(d) Cedric Durand or Piketty on financialization and inequality. (e) Alice Amsden on the East Asian developmental state (1990).
