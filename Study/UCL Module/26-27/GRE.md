# GRE General Test: Complete Guide and Prep Timeline

## Part 1: GRE Exam Overview

### What is the GRE

GRE (Graduate Record Examination) is a standardized test administered by ETS (Educational Testing Service), widely accepted by graduate programs in the US, UK, Canada, and Australia. JHU Political Science PhD still requires the GRE General Test as a mandatory component of the application. Scores remain valid for five years from the test date.

### Test Format

The current GRE General Test (updated September 2023) is a computer-based, section-level adaptive exam. "Section-level adaptive" means that the difficulty of the second section in Verbal and Quantitative depends on your performance in the first section of each measure. Performing well in the first section routes you to a harder second section, which in turn unlocks a higher scoring range. This design makes the first section of each measure especially important.

The test consists of five sections in total, always beginning with Analytical Writing. After the writing section, Verbal Reasoning and Quantitative Reasoning sections appear in any order.

### Duration

Total test time is approximately 1 hour 58 minutes, with no scheduled breaks. This is substantially shorter than the pre-2023 format, which lasted 3 hours 45 minutes.

The time breakdown is as follows. Analytical Writing has one section lasting 30 minutes. Verbal Reasoning has two sections, where Section 1 gives 18 minutes for 12 questions and Section 2 gives 23 minutes for 15 questions. Quantitative Reasoning also has two sections, where Section 1 gives 21 minutes for 12 questions and Section 2 gives 26 minutes for 15 questions.

### Scoring

Verbal Reasoning and Quantitative Reasoning are each scored on a scale of 130 to 170, in 1-point increments. These two scores add up to a combined total ranging from 260 to 340. Analytical Writing is scored separately on a 0 to 6 scale in half-point increments and does not factor into the combined total. The AW essay is evaluated by both a human reader and an automated scoring engine, with a third reader brought in if the two scores differ by more than one point. There is no negative marking on the GRE, so leaving questions unanswered is always worse than guessing.

For reference, the mean scores among recent test takers are approximately 151 for Verbal, 155 for Quantitative, and 3.5 for Analytical Writing.

### Test Location

There are two options, both producing identical scores with no indicator on the score report of which format was used.

The first option is testing at an ETS-authorized Prometric test center. Centers are available in over 160 countries, and in most regions, tests are offered on a continuous basis throughout the year. In London, there are multiple Prometric test centers. The second option is testing at home (GRE at Home), which is available 24/7 year-round. The at-home test uses a personal computer monitored by a human proctor via webcam. As of January 2026, ETS requires a second camera device (smartphone or tablet) positioned to show a side-angle view of your workspace. As of March 2026, all at-home test takers must complete identity verification through the IDVaaS app at least 72 hours before the test.

The test can be taken up to five times in any rolling 12-month period, with a minimum gap of 21 days between attempts. Official scores are typically available 8 to 10 days after the test date.

### Registration and Cost

Registration is done online through your ETS account. The fee is $220 globally. Rescheduling costs approximately $55 and must be done at least four full days before the test date. For test center appointments, registering 3 to 4 months in advance is recommended to secure preferred slots.

## Part 2: Question Types and Key Strategies

### Analytical Writing (1 task, 30 minutes)

The AW section presents one "Analyze an Issue" task. You will be given a statement on a broad topic and asked to write an essay presenting your position with evidence and reasoning. (The pre-2023 "Analyze an Argument" task has been removed.) This section evaluates your ability to articulate complex ideas clearly, support claims with relevant evidence, and organize an essay coherently.

Key strategies from the GRE All-In-One repo author (who scored 3.5 with minimal prep): The AW topic pool is fixed and publicly available on the ETS website. If time permits, preparing outlines for topics in advance guarantees encountering a familiar prompt on test day. For target scores of 4.0 or above, practicing 5 to 10 timed essays is recommended.

For your situation specifically: your academic writing experience (LSE abstract, TAI policy analysis, Sciences Po coursework) already exceeds the demand of GRE AW. The key constraint is the 30-minute time limit and the essay structure conventions, not writing ability itself.

### Verbal Reasoning (2 sections, 27 questions total)

Verbal Reasoning evaluates reading comprehension, vocabulary in context, and logical analysis of written material. The question types are as follows.

Text Completion (TC) presents a passage of 1 to 5 sentences with 1 to 3 blanks, where you select the word that best fits each blank from a set of answer choices. Sentence Equivalence (SE) presents one sentence with a single blank and six answer choices, where you must select two words that both complete the sentence and produce sentences alike in meaning. Reading Comprehension (RC) presents passages of varying lengths (short, medium, or long) followed by multiple-choice questions about the passage. Each Verbal section typically begins with Text Completion questions, followed by the other types in varying order.

Key strategies from the repo: Vocabulary is the single most important factor for Verbal. The repo author used the "再要你命3000" (Zaiyo Timing 3000) word list as the primary resource and considered it sufficient for scoring above 330 combined. The "红宝书" (GRE Vocabulary Masterwork by New Oriental) contains additional words, but some are excessively obscure and offer low return for short-term preparation. For memorization method, the author recommends Yang Peng's "17 Days to Conquer GRE Vocabulary," which uses a systematic review schedule based on the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve. For practice, the author strongly recommends using online platforms like Kmf (考满分) for Text Completion drilling with authentic test items.

The other critical factor is long and complex sentence parsing. GRE passages frequently use multi-layered sentence structures that are significantly more difficult than IELTS reading. The repo recommends Yang Peng's "GRE Long and Difficult Sentences" (100 sentences total, 20 per day) to build this skill.

For reading strategy, the repo author used a method of close reading the full passage without looking back, then answering questions from memory, which requires training but produces strong results under time pressure. The recommended time allocation is 10 to 12 minutes on all Text Completion questions first, then remaining time on Reading Comprehension.

### Quantitative Reasoning (2 sections, 27 questions total)

Quantitative Reasoning tests mathematical problem-solving using concepts at approximately the high-school level. The question types are as follows.

Quantitative Comparison (QC) presents two quantities (A and B) and asks you to determine which is greater, or whether the relationship cannot be determined. This type always appears at the start of each Quant section. Multiple Choice (one or more answers) presents standard problem-solving questions. Numeric Entry requires typing in a numerical answer rather than selecting from choices.

The content areas include arithmetic, algebra, geometry, and data analysis (statistics, probability, reading charts and tables). An on-screen calculator is available.

Key strategies from the repo: For someone with an economics quantitative background, the math content is well below the difficulty of UCL coursework. The repo recommends "巍哥GRE数学170难题3.0" (Wei's GRE Math 170 Hard Questions 3.0) as a single resource to work through, focusing on understanding error patterns. The main risk in Quant is carelessness and trick questions rather than conceptual difficulty.
