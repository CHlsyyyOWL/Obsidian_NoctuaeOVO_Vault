段落一：以国际发展政治经济的理论为起点，引入 de facto power 概念）

The relationship between historical political structures and economic outcomes has long been a central focus in both comparative politics and political economy. 

Foundational work by Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001) emphasizes the important role of institution in economic development, and argues that political institutions alters the distribution of de jure political power, but creates incentives for investments in de facto political power to partially or even fully offset change in de jure power. Their studies in 2008 further distinguished between de jure political power endowed from formal rules and constitutions, and _de facto_ power, exercised through social capitals and informal authority. According to their theory, de facto power shapes how formal institutions are implemented, thus has greater explanatory power for long-term economic development that relates to policy decisions and resources allocations. 


段落二：革命遗产中的干部本地化与制度惯性**

After 1949, China’s revolutionary war and early state-building left a clear institutional legacy. 
This variation in cadre composition was not random; it closely reflected the distribution of _de facto_ political power during the war period. Historically, the proportion of locally born cadres in the provincial leadership was closely affected by the lasting effects of the Civil War. In areas where revolutionary bases were established during the War, the share of local cadres was usually high. As shown in Figure 1, provinces such as Hebei, Shandong, Shanxi, and western Shaanxi—all of which had revolutionary bases—had local cadre shares above 80%. In contrast, places that were under the Chinese Nationalist Party's control or more isolated, such as coastal regions, inland provinces, and areas like Yunnan, Guizhou, and Qinghai, had zero local cadres in their first committees. In this way, early patterns of cadre assignment signaled how political authority was rooted in grassroots revolutionary mobilization.

Importantly, these initial power structures did not disappear with institutional changes, but they tended to reproduce themselves over time. In areas with revolutionary bases, local cadres had gone through wartime and built strong organizational and military capacity, as well as gained trust from the central government. During the Civil War, local armed forces often liberated these regions, and locals mostly managed the new governments. In contrast, external military units usually liberated areas without such a history, and their governments were taken over by cadres from outside. The structure of the first provincial committee that was formed through political bargaining reflected the distribution of grassroots political power. A higher share of local cadres meant that local grassroots forces held more _de facto_ political power.

More importantly, this initial power structure tended to persist and strengthen itself over time. In China’s political system, personal networks play a key role in promotion. When local cadres dominate, they often promote those with similar backgrounds or prior work connections. These ties—initially built through work—can be extended into family or social relations, such as kinship, marriage, hometown, or school networks. These informal networks then expand in both depth and scope, and can even be passed down across generations. This self-reinforcing mechanism makes the original structure of _de facto_ power more stable, although increasingly hidden and complex.

Empirical work by Landry (2008) provides evidence for how this structure became embedded in formal rules, by showing how the CCP institutionalized cadre localization through a “one-level-down” promotion system, which allowed local Party committees to appoint officials within their own jurisdictions. This reinforced the dominance of local networks and made vertical supervision from the center more difficult. Although China is officially a unitary state, such arrangements produced a system of “decentralized authoritarianism,” where local officials exercised considerable _de facto_ power. Later studies confirm the persistence of this structure. Landry and Liu (2011) find that even in the reform era, provincial leaders still showed strong home-origin preferences in cadre selection. Edin (2003) similarly finds that local cadre management remained embedded in personal ties and faced ongoing principal–agent problems. These findings align with the idea that revolutionary-era political arrangements, particularly those that empowered local actors, created long-lasting informal institutions that continued to shape governance styles and regional policy outcomes. Together, this literature suggests that cadre localization is both a product of wartime politics and a key mechanism through which _de facto_ political power endures.
  
Beyond political structure, cadre localization also has important implications for regional economic development. Important consequences for regional development. Mahoney (2000) argues that institutional “lock-in” happens when initial arrangements create self-reinforcing paths over time. In China, cadre localization may lead to governance practices that favor administrative stability and political loyalty, rather than economic experimentation or coordination across provinces. Huang (1996) notes that during the reform era, subnational governments gained increasing control over economic affairs, which made the background and incentives of local officials more important. When resource allocation and policy implementation depend more on informal local networks than on central plans, the composition of local elites becomes a key factor. Recent empirical studies support this view. Sun et al. (2022) show that _de facto_ political power is a stronger predictor of resource outcomes than _de jure_ rules in transitional China. Ang (2016) finds that clientelist networks, not formal institutions, often determine who gets access to development resources. Similarly, Su and Heurlin (2019) argue that in authoritarian systems, local responsiveness comes more from informal flexibility than from merit-based rules.

 Despite these theoretical and empirical insights, one question remains unclear: What are the long-term economic consequences of early cadre localization? While these studies have improved our understanding of China’s decentralized governance, one important question remains open: how has revolutionary-era cadre localization shaped long-term regional economic outcomes? Scholars like Montinola, Qian, and Weingast (1995) describe “Chinese federalism” as a system where provinces have policy autonomy under a unified political structure. Others, such as Xu (2011), emphasize how regional competition drives growth. However, few works empirically connect early cadre deployment patterns to differences in regional development in later decades. Likewise, studies of state-building (Slater 2010; Sinha 2007) highlight how early institutional choices shape later outcomes, but they often ignore how the _composition_ of local governing elites influences these paths in authoritarian settings like China.

This paper aims to fill that gap by testing whether the share of locally born cadres in provincial governments, as a proxy for de facto power, has a long-term effect on economic performance. Using institutional theory, principal–agent models, and the logic of path dependence, the paper argues that cadre localization weakens vertical coordination, reduces the incentives for growth-focused governance, and contributes to regional inequality. By linking the cadre structure of the 1950s to GDP outcomes in the 1960s, the study provides historical evidence for how revolutionary institutions continue to shape China’s political economy today.
