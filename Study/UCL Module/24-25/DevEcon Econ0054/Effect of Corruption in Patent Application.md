Literature review:

The relationship between corruption and firm innovation remains debated between the “grease the wheels” and “sand the wheels” hypotheses. Some argue that bribery helps firms bypass bureaucratic obstacles and compensate for weak political connections, facilitating innovation in emerging markets (Kramme, 2017). However, growing evidence suggests that corruption undermines innovation, particularly in process innovation and among smaller firms (Wellalage & Thrikawala, 2021; Poddar & Singh, 2022). While Viglioni et al. (2022) acknowledge that corruption may boost short-term financial performance, they find it weakens the impact of R&D investments, harming long-term innovation.

Institutional quality plays a crucial role in shaping these effects. In weak institutional environments, corruption may temporarily ease regulatory burdens, but firms risk becoming dependent on informal, rent-seeking practices rather than genuine innovation (Viglioni et al., 2022; Wellalage & Thrikawala, 2021). Conversely, in stronger institutional settings, corruption’s negative impact is more evident, as transparent governance reduces firms’ reliance on bribery (Poddar & Singh, 2022). Overall, while some studies highlight short-term benefits, the prevailing view supports the “sand the wheels” hypothesis, as corruption ultimately stifles innovation, especially where institutions are weak.


## Discussion
**Institution**
  
Our regression results reveal a clear and statistically significant negative relationship between corruption and innovation, as measured by the Corruption Perceptions Index (CPI) and patent applications per million inhabitants. Countries with higher perceived corruption—indicated by lower CPI scores—tend to generate fewer patent applications when controlling for key development indicators such as GDP per capita, tertiary education, financial access, and government expenditure. This supports the “**sanding the wheels**” hypothesis, suggesting that corruption as an indicator of institutional weakness—deters innovation and development.

The potential mechanism behind can be explained by **institutional economics**. Corruption is not just a governance issue, bur an institutional barrier to development. In environments where formal institutions are weak and corruption is prevalent, the rules of the game are shaped less by legal norms and more by the interests of those in power. This leads to increased **uncertainty and transaction costs** for individuals and firms. Innovators, in particular, face difficulties in securing intellectual property rights and may turn to informal or illegal strategies to minimize costs. These distortions act as a **tax on innovation**, discouraging long-term investments in research and development.t. 

This logic reflects the idea of "extractive institutions"(Acemoglu and Robinson, 2012)—systems where power and resources are controlled by a few, disincentivizing innovation. Therefore, corruption becomes a tool to maintain control, as firms focus on building political connections or paying bribes instead of investing in research. This shift of resources away from productive use is especially harmful in developing countries, where innovation already faces barriers like weak infrastructure, limited funding, and poor education systems. n this context, strengthening institutions becomes essential not only for reducing corruption but also for unlocking the broader developmental benefits of innovation.
