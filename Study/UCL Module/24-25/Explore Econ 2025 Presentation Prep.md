Why are we researching this?
Before we start our methodology, let's look at some interesting numbers -----

(Page 1) 9% Colombia
 In Colombia, only **9%** of stock market assets are traded each year, 
 meaning that among **100 assets** waiting to be sold, only **9** of them can find a buyer. 
 
 Well, How about this in Germany?

(Page 2) 79% Germany 
 It is 79%. 

(Page 3) Ben S Bernanke 
 I believe you can find that Colombian people's assets are illiquid, or in other words, to some extent, they suffer credit constraint.

As Ben Bernanke said, “Credit is the lifeblood of market economies.” If credit cannot flow due to illiquid assets, as we conclude in our paper, then the economy’s responsiveness to policy is weakened.

***How damage is this? How is credit constraint related to policy effectiveness?***

(Page 4)
 We narrow down this research question by decomposing the credit constraint and the collateral channel of monetary policy, and we ask:

 How do credit constraints affect the effectiveness of the collateral channel, especially in emerging market economies?

 How will credit constraints influence the collateral channel? We argue that, as shown in the graph, when policy interest rate change as a result of monetary policy, the asset price will then be influenced, however, not all asset can be sold in their price, as the example of Colombia: if the overall market has a low turn over rate, which means no one is buying your high value asset, then you would have to lower your price, or you get nothing.

 The lost between the asset price from your market, and what you **really get on hand** after liquidation, is determined by the extent of credit constraint, and is therefore decomposed into four variables:

(Page 5) 

 •the Loan-to-Value Ratio, Miu 

 •the Liquidation Cost, Theta

 •Economic Uncertainty, Gamma

 •Financial Development Index 

 Our four key variables influence consumption through this specified consumption function. This function conveys the characteristics and variations in each variable that determine the level of consumption of an economy. 

(Page 14)
 After all the complicated math work, 
 **How does our theory apply to the real-world emerging market economy**?

 **Back to the beginning**, where we mentioned the drastic disparity between Colombia and Germany, 
 In the case study:
 We **collected** and **visualized** more detailed data on their economies based on our four variables. 
 To collect more representative data on economic uncertainty, we used the credit default swap spread index to represent this variable.
         **The credit default swap (CDS)** spread index measures the cost of insuring a country’s debt against default
         Higher spreads mean higher perceived credit risk.

(Page 15)
 As we could see

 Colombia scores low in financial development, with a 0.39 Financial Development Index,  has high credit risk with a shocking Hundred and fourty-nine,  and enforces a stricter LTV limit of 80%. This creates a more constrained credit environment.

 In contrast, Germany’s developed financial system ,with a 0.71 Financial development index, a low credit risk of only 15 Credit default swap spread,  and relaxed LTV rules, allows for more asset-based borrowing.

(Page 16)
 The difference has resulted in a great disparity in how their consumption responds to housing price changes as a result of interest rate changes

 In Colombia, the trends of housing prices and consumer spending diverge, as we can see they nearly move in the opposite direction; 
 But in Germany, the two move more closely, suggesting that asset price changes translate more effectively into consumption.

(Page 17)
 At the end, how important is credit constraints in the collateral channel? 
 Tighter credit constraints,
 ---which is driven by high liquidation costs, low LTV limits, low financial development, and high uncertainty, 
 would significantly weaken the collateral channel in emerging markets.

 To enhance policy effectiveness, we recommend reforms that improve asset liquidity, reduce risk perceptions, and ease borrowing conditions.
