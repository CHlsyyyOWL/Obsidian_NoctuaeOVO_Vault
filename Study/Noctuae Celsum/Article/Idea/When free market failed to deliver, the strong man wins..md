[[经济人的末日]] [[极权主义可能卷土重来吗？]]

 "We don't want bread cheap, we don't want bread dear, we don't want bread unchanged — we want National Socialist bread." The crowds roared. They knew it was nonsense. They cheered anyway.

Fascism was not the product of propaganda, big business conspiracy, or primitive barbarism. It was the product of a promise broken twice. Capitalism had promised freedom through prosperity. Marxism had promised liberation from capitalism. Both failed. In the vacuum of that double failure, people did not become sceptics. They became desperate — eager for anyone to say _no_ loudly enough to fill the silence where hope used to be.

Eighty-seven years later, that silence is returning.

**The first signal is economic.** Real wages in Britain remain, after inflation, 2% below their 2008 level. For most working-age adults, seventeen years of nominal pay rises have produced virtually no real gain. Between November 2021 and June 2023, inflation outpaced wages for twenty consecutive months — the longest sustained real-wage fall in modern British history. The average first-time buyer in England is now 34. In London, 35. Without family money, a London deposit runs close to £150,000. Energy bills sit 44% above pre-crisis levels with no credible path back. These are not inconveniences. They are the precise conditions under which people stop believing the system works for them.

Across the Atlantic, the picture is uglier. Median real wages for American workers without a college degree are lower today than in 1979. The bottom half of the income distribution has seen essentially no productivity gains pass through to their pay in forty years. Opioid deaths, deaths of despair, collapsed towns — the social wreckage of a market that delivered for capital and credential but not for labour. This is the America that elected Donald Trump twice.

**The second signal is political.** When the left stops challenging the system and starts administering it, it surrenders the ground to something worse.

European Marxism did not fall to external attack. It transformed itself  by succeeding. As unions grew powerful and won real concessions, socialism became syndicalism: no longer seeking to replace capitalism but to claim its largest share. The revolutionary became a stakeholder. A movement that defends the order it once fought cannot be the refuge for those the order has abandoned.

Britain's Labour Party has followed this path precisely. The biggest legislative shift in employment law in decades, the Employment Rights Act 2025, has made hiring more expensive and legally treacherous, while payroll employment fell and vacancies dropped below pre-pandemic levels. Its green industrial strategy commits billions to mandated investment in technologies the market has not chosen. It speaks the language of workers while governing for a managed capitalism in which the state sets every term. It is syndicalism in a Savile Row suit.

One may ask, what fills the vacancy Labour leaves?  It is the politics of pure negation. 

No positive programme. No coherent vision. Just opposition. Opposition to everything, in all directions simultaneously. Trump's platform is instructive: anti-free trade and anti-regulation, anti-immigration and anti-establishment, against the wars and against the weakness. His tariffs benefit no identifiable constituency. His promises contradict each other. His supporters know this. They cheer anyway. You don't need a programme if you have an enemy, and even you don't need to know who you are, as long as you know who you are against.

**The third signal is structural.** Both parties in Britain are converging on the same response: more state. Labour adds regulation and mandate. Reform would add tariffs and immigration controls. Neither asks whether the market failed because it was genuinely free, or because it was never allowed to be.

Housing supply is throttled by planning law written to protect incumbent owners. Energy markets are distorted beyond recognition by subsidy, mandate, and political risk premium. Labour markets are made rigid by regulations that protect insiders at the cost of those trying to enter. When markets produce these outcomes, and governments respond with further intervention rather than genuine liberalisation, they do not merely make the economics worse. They validate the argument that individual agency is futile — that no amount of work, qualification, or prudence can secure a stable life.

Hannah Arendt called this the seedbed of totalitarianism. Neither poverty nor inequality, but loneliness, the atomisation of people who have lost connection to shared institutions, common facts, and each other. Totalitarian movements recruit the isolated, offering an ideology certain enough to fill every gap. Platforms optimised for engagement produce outrage, tribal identity, and epistemic isolation at industrial scale. Millions connected to a movement, disconnected from their neighbours. Arendt's atomised European between the wars had his world destroyed by industrial capitalism and failed revolution. His descendant has his world narrowed by a feed.

Nobody is stopping the spread of silence. Housing that excludes a generation. Energy costs that have not recovered. A labour market hostile to new entrants. A political opposition defined entirely by what it is against. The answer is not a larger state to compensate for market failure. It is the unglamorous work of making markets actually function: build where people need to live, price energy without mandating the technology, let new workers enter labour markets that incumbents have captured...for a population that has stopped believing in the possibility of individual agency is the most dangerous political condition there is.

The bread price was nonsense. Everyone knew it. They cheered anyway.

We have been warned.
