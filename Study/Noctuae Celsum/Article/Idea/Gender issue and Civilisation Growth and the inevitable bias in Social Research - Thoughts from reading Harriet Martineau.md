xHarriet Martineau in her book using vivic descriptions to illustrate the importance of having a scientific and impartial approach to observe the society, as academic work is different from a traveller's notes and should be done rigoursly. She also proposed the harm of bias if conduct social research in a casual way. She emphasises the necessary preparation before starting a sociological research - research question "a man should know what he is wanting to know" and the methodology (the Deaf Traveller example), as well as research background investigation. 

In the second part of her book, as she firmly propose that one's moral will be reflected through one's manner, she list out the most frequent social topics and explain in detail one by one about what to observe for the good of research, serving as a guidence for social observations in general. 

At last, she stated the requirments for one to be physically and mentally, apart from the intellectual requirements stated above, to be qualified for social research. Such as patience, healthy etc. 

I found 2 points in her book interesting. One is her standard in measuring the progress of a country - how they treat women. I believe this idea is still prominent in today's gender studies and being used by feminists in social platforms. On the other side, this reminds me of women's situation in most Asia countries, that is South Korea, Japan, India, and China. None of the places, women are receiving a ideal wealfare as expected，both in terms of the labor market and in the culture in general. Women in Japan are expected to become a good women still, not expecting them to work and earn as equal as man did. Women in South Korea had started many protests for female rights, but less effect was observed. Apart from these two developed countries, for the rest two developing countries, female rights and gender equality have not even become an issue to be considered in these to countries, with people consider it natural for women to be biased and receive low wage in the labor market. In contrast, there was a time in the history, that women are receiving.equal rights as men did in Japan and South Korea. Interestingly, both were during the period when the 2 countries were experiencing rapid economic growth - the cake is large enough for the men to  generously share one piece to the women, or it is because the profit motivated firms to ignore the gender issue and treat all people euqal for greater proft. 

After what is said and done, although I might have bias when observing and generalizing the gender issue in Asian countries, my question arise: If the extent of civilisation progress can be truely reflected by a society's treatment to women ( instead of "treatment to women" is only an issue to affected by culture and geographical factor), then why hardly we observe this kind of progress in the four Asian countries? This seems to reflect the fact that economic progress do not bring permanent progress in its civilisation level (only temporarily, and it is not true progress but a compromise from men in favour for more money), then what is the determinant factor for civilisation progress?

Another interesting thing to mention is the idea of "impartial" in observation - unlike Durkheim, she is not pure positivist as far as I am concerned, as she did not deny the fact that social research have the natural mission to "judge" the society, in order to improve the society. But here seems to be too ideal, that "observation" itself, as well as the fundemental bricks for research - expression, hence language - can hardly be impartial, according to Habermas，“social sciences involve subjects and objects sharing the same social world”， and the way researcher observing and expressing them in language, naturally reflect their culture background, as "Every language provides its speakers with a particular worldview"(Wilheim Von Homboldt) and the Sapir–Whorf Hypothesis of language restricted by thought itself. My questions arises, that Martineau only mentioned the importance of "impartial" (ofc I did not read the full book I am sorry), but I consider these minor prejudice from culture and language are inevitable - is it truly inevitable, if so how do we deal with them, both when ourselves conducing social research, and when we are reading other's academic research report?
