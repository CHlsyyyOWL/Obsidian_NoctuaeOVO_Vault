### Early European Dynamics of Growth

Adam Smith "The Wealth of Nations," pointed out that the driving force to economic growth is productivity gains attending division of labor and specialization. As the market expands, the opportunity for Smithian growth increases accordingly. 

- Smithian Growth: Extensive growth, based on market expansion and better allocation of existing resources.
- Schumpeterian: Intensive growth, driven by innovation, technological change, and “creative destruction.”
  
- Many economic historians (e.g., Eric Jones, Douglass North, Kenneth Pomeranz) see pre-industrial Eurasia as primarily **Smithian** in growth pattern—commercial expansion without sustained technological revolution.

1348-50 Black Death in the Mediterranean to northern Europe
- Hadicraft production disrupted
- Trade between the cities declined.
- Urban demand for agricultural goods fell. 

16th-century gradual recovery to a higher level of total population and total output
- New financial infrastructure supporting long-distance trade with the elaboration of banking and marketing institutions enabling sophisticated division of labor (mobility).
- Ernest Labrousse (1990) marked the rhythm of the pre-19th-century European economy:
   "However, it relies on a fragile economic base on agriculture; harvests determine the annual fluctuation of food prices, thus the whole economy is triggered by a cyclical downturn."
- Although with the flourish of the textile industry with new marketing networks developed, the economic growth in mainly Holland and England looks impressive, taking the overall average of Europe, where some countries in the meantime is experiencing recession, the economic growth rate in Europe seems modest

17th Century, economies grew in the early modern period, mainly through:
- extended arable
- increased inputs of K and L
- increased specialization
- technological growth 
Since agricultural production had grown so large, economies had little ability to grow faster than the expansion of agricultural land. In 17th-century Europe, the "general crisis" brought up by **Malthusian** came into place in economic, social, and political difficulties. 

18th Century, Western Europe increasingly engaged in commerce through agriculture and industry.
- England escaped recurring famine threats
- France no longer faced subsistence crises of earlier magnitudes. 
The Europe Smith analyzed was better off than before, while it remained far from the 19th-century urban factory industrialisation that led to the fundamental transformation. Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations remained an agricultural economy, since it emphasizes the importance of agricultural investment and the finite of economic expansion. 

Malthusian and Smith lived in the 18th century, not the 19th century, a time when European Society had more in common with China at that period than Europe in the 19th century.

#### Industrial Revolution and Involution - De Vries view(1993,1994)

**De Vries pointed out a demand side shift before the supply-side change that accounted for industrial revolution:**
- Between 1600 and 1800 increasing numbers of Europeans, including peasants, became **dependent** upon market purchases.
    - This demand stimulated market expansion at a time when **per capita incomes were not rising**, and indeed may have even been falling.
- This seems counterintuitive. Why did people buy and sell more goods if their wages did not increase?
    - De Vries explains that Europeans began to **work harder**; they became more "industrious." As evidence he cites the reduction of nonworking days for both artisans and peasants, and he suggests that people became more willing to trade leisure for money.” (Wong, 1997, p. 30)
