# Speaker Notes

## Slide 1: Why Zhejiang vs Liaoning

Our research asks what factors shape how provinces implement credit repair under the new national framework. We chose Zhejiang and Liaoning because they represent two opposite responses to the same set of national regulations.

Both provinces are required to follow three national documents that came out in 2025. The State Council issued a general notice in June setting overall timelines. Then in November, two ministries issued their own regulations almost simultaneously. NDRC Order No. 36 covers all categories of credit information across all sectors, while SAMR Order No. 107 covers only market supervision categories. These two tracks operate in parallel rather than in sequence, which already creates room for provinces to respond differently depending on which track they follow and how they interpret overlapping requirements.

Zhejiang stands out because it enacted its own provincial regulation, Decree No. 406, in March 2025, six months before the NDRC framework was even published. It also built a unified digital platform called Zhelibàn that integrates the entire credit repair process from application to decision. This makes Zhejiang a policy innovator rather than an implementer of central directives.

Liaoning presents the opposite picture. As of 2026, Liaoning has not enacted any provincial credit repair legislation. But it does have real operational activity. Over 65,000 entities completed credit repair in 2025 through SAMR channels, and both Dalian and Shenyang are running municipal pilot programs. So in Liaoning, credit repair is happening in practice without a provincial legal framework to structure it.

This contrast is what makes the comparison useful. One province legislates ahead of the centre and builds infrastructure. The other operates without legislation and relies on municipal experiments. The question is what this divergence tells us about how provincial institutions actually work.

## Slide 2: Method

To analyse this divergence, we adapted an existing framework from Ma (2024), who originally used it to study China's household registration reform. Ma built on Abbott et al.'s legalization theory from international law and simplified it into two dimensions, obligation and enforcement.

Obligation measures how binding the policy is. We look at whether a province issued formal legislation or just guidance documents, whether the language is mandatory or just encouraging, whether eligibility conditions are spelled out, and whether there are sanctions for non-compliance. A province that enacted a decree with legal force scores higher on obligation than one relying on national documents by default.

Enforcement measures whether the policy can actually be carried out as written. We look at whether there is a digital platform that standardises processing, whether there are fixed timelines with named responsible departments, whether data is shared across agencies, and whether there are monitoring or accountability mechanisms.

Crossing these two dimensions gives us four types. Type 4 means both obligation and enforcement are strong, which is where we placed Zhejiang's Decree No. 406 alongside SAMR Order No. 107 at the national level. Type 1 means both are weak, which is where Liaoning's provincial response falls because it has no statute and no enforcement infrastructure specific to credit repair. Type 2 means enforcement is strong but obligation is weak. NDRC Order No. 36 falls here at the national level, and so do the municipal pilots in Hangzhou, Dalian, and Shenyang, because they accelerate processing without creating binding standards. Type 3 would mean strong obligation but weak enforcement, and we found no cases in this cell.

We coded policy documents at three administrative levels, national, provincial, and municipal, on six indicators. These are issuer rank, target specificity, instrument specificity, substantive criteria specificity, platform infrastructure, and coordination scope.

## Slide 3: Key Findings

The comparison table summarises our coding results across five dimensions.

We classified each province by asking two questions about their credit repair policies. First, does the policy create binding legal obligations? Second, are there working systems to make sure the policy is carried out? A province that scores high on both is Type 4, while a province that scores low on both is Type 1.

Zhejiang falls in Type 4 because it passed a provincial decree with legal force and built a digital platform that tracks every repair application. The decree specifies who handles the application, within what timeline, and through which platform. Liaoning falls in Type 1 because it passed no provincial credit repair law and has no province-wide digital platform. Its municipal pilots in Dalian and Shenyang speed up processing times but do not set standards for what qualifies as a successful repair.

On the municipal level, both provinces show innovation but in different directions. Hangzhou added an "execution observation period" that creates a waiting stage between when a violation is identified and when enforcement begins, giving applicants time to demonstrate correction. Dalian and Shenyang compressed the processing timeline from 15 working days to as little as 24 hours, but they did not change the criteria for deciding whether an applicant qualifies.

The key finding is about what neither province does. Despite this difference in formal infrastructure, neither Zhejiang nor Liaoning defines in its policy documents what counts as "having corrected the violation." This is the phrase that determines whether a credit repair application gets approved or rejected. Because it is left undefined, the official reviewing each case still decides on a case-by-case basis whether the applicant qualifies. Zhejiang wraps this judgment inside a regulated process with fixed timelines and digital tracking, while Liaoning leaves it in an environment with no provincial procedural framework at all.

This pattern is consistent with what Ma (2024) found in household registration reform. In that case, formal hardening of policy at higher administrative levels coexisted with open-ended discretion at the implementation level. Our contribution is showing that the same pattern appears in credit repair, a different policy domain, and that it holds even when provinces start from very different institutional positions.
