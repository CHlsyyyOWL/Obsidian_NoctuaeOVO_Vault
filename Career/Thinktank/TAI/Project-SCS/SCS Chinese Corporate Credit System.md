## 基本信息

### 什么是Social Credit System?
- 基于2014年由中华人民共和国国务院印发的[《国务院关于印发社会信用体系建设规划纲要（2014—2020年）的通知》](https://www.gov.cn/zhengce/content/2014-06/27/content_8913.htm)，计划到2020年基本建成、奖惩机制全面启用。该项政策旨在建设涵盖政府机构、企业、个人的全社会征信体系 
#### - 政务诚信
- 政务公开
	- 提高决策透明度。进一步推广重大决策事项公示和听证制度，拓宽公众参与政府决策的渠道
	- 加强对权力运行的社会监督和约束，提升政府公信力，树立政府公开、公平、清廉的诚信形象
	- 在行政许可、政府采购、招标投标、劳动就业、社会保障、科研管理、干部选拔任用和管理监督、申请政府资金支持等领域，率先使用信用信息和信用产品，
	- 政务履约和守诺服务
	- 强化预算约束,提高透明度
	- 群众监督与舆论监督
#### - 商务诚信
- 生产信用建设
	- 营商环境
	- 安全生产信用
	- 生产质量信用信息异地部门之间共享制度
	- 举报平台
	- 质量诚信报告 
	- 失信黑名单披露
	- 市场禁入退出制度
- 流通信用
	- 推进批发零售、商贸物流、住宿餐饮及居民服务行业信用建设
	- 开展企业信用分类管理。
	- 完善零售商与供应商信用合作模式。
	- 强化反垄断与反不正当竞争执法，加大对市场混淆行为、虚假宣传、商业欺诈、商业诋毁、商业贿赂等违法行为的查处力度，
	- 对典型案件、重大案件予以曝光，增加企业失信成本，促进诚信经营和公平竞争。逐步建立以商品条形码等标识为基础的全国商品流通追溯体系。
	- 加强检验检疫质量诚信体系建设。支持商贸服务企业信用融资，发展商业保理，规范预付消费行为。
	- 鼓励企业扩大信用销售，促进个人信用消费。
	- 推进对外经济贸易信用建设，进一步加强对外贸易、对外援助、对外投资合作等领域的信用信息管理、信用风险监测预警和企业信用等级分类管理。
	- 借助电子口岸管理平台，建立完善进出口企业信用评价体系、信用分类管理和联合监管制度。
- 金融信用
	- 创新金融信用产品，改善金融服务，维护金融消费者个人信息安全，保护金融消费者合法权益。
	- 加大对金融欺诈、恶意逃废银行债务、内幕交易、制售假保单、骗保骗赔、披露虚假信息、非法集资、逃套骗汇等金融失信行为的惩戒力度，
	- 规范金融市场秩序。加强金融信用信息基础设施建设，
	- 进一步扩大信用记录的覆盖面，强化金融业对守信者的激励作用和对失信者的约束作用。
- 税务信用
	- 开展纳税人基础信息、各类交易信息、财产保有和转让信息以及纳税记录等涉税信息的交换、比对和应用工作。
	- 进一步完善纳税信用等级评定和发布制度，加强税务领域信用分类管理，发挥信用评定差异对纳税人的奖惩作用。
	- 建立税收违法黑名单制度。推进纳税信用与其他社会信用联动管理，提升纳税人税法遵从度
- 价格信用
	- 指导企业和经营者加强价格自律，规范和引导经营者价格行为，
	- 实行经营者明码标价和收费公示制度，着力推行“明码实价”。督促经营者加强内部价格管理，根据经营者条件建立健全内部价格管理制度。完
	- 善经营者价格诚信制度，做好信息披露工作，推动实施奖惩制度。
	- 强化价格执法检查与反垄断执法，依法查处捏造和散布涨价信息、价格欺诈、价格垄断等价格失信行为，对典型案例予以公开曝光，规范市场价格秩序。
- 工程建设信用
	- 推进工程建设市场信用体系建设。加快工程建设市场信用法规制度建设，制定工程建设市场各方主体和从业人员信用标准。
	- 推进工程建设领域项目信息公开和诚信体系建设，依托政府网站，全面设立项目信息和信用信息公开共享专栏，集中公开工程建设项目信息和信用信息，推动建设全国性的综合检索平台，实现工程建设项目信息和信用信息公开共享的“一站式”综合检索服务。
	- 深入开展工程质量诚信建设。完善工程建设市场准入退出制度，加大对发生重大工程质量、安全责任事故或有其他重大失信行为的企业及负有责任的从业人员的惩戒力度。
	- 建立企业和从业人员信用评价结果与资质审批、执业资格注册、资质资格取消等审批审核事项的关联管理机制。建立科学、有效的建设领域从业人员信用评价机制和失信责任追溯制度，将肢解发包、转包、违法分包、拖欠工程款和农民工工资等列入失信责任追究范围
- 政府采购信用
	- 加强政府采购信用管理，强化联动惩戒，保护政府采购当事人的合法权益。
	- 制定供应商、评审专家、政府采购代理机构以及相关从业人员的信用记录标准。依法建立政府采购供应商不良行为记录名单，对列入不良行为记录名单的供应商，在一定期限内禁止参加政府采购活动。完
	- 善政府采购市场的准入和退出机制，充分利用工商、税务、金融、检察等其他部门提供的信用信息，加强对政府采购当事人和相关人员的信用管理。
	- 加快建设全国统一的政府采购管理交易系统，提高政府采购活动透明度，实现信用信息的统一发布和共享
- 招标投标信用
	- 扩大招标投标信用信息公开和共享范围，建立涵盖招标投标情况的信用评价指标和评价标准体系，健全招标投标信用信息公开和共享制度。进一步贯彻落实招标投标违法行为记录公告制度，推动完善奖惩联动机制。依托电子招标投标系统及其公共服务平台，实现招标投标和合同履行等信用信息的互联互通、实时交换和整合共享。鼓励市场主体运用基本信用信息和第三方信用评价结果，并将其作为投标人资格审查、评标、定标和合同签订的重要依据。
- 交通运输信用
	- 形成部门规章制度和地方性法规、地方政府规章相结合的交通运输信用法规体系。完善信用考核标准，实施分类考核监管。针对公路、铁路、水路、民航、管道等运输市场不同经营门类分别制定考核指标，加强信用考核评价监督管理，积极引导第三方机构参与信用考核评价，逐步建立交通运输管理机构与社会信用评价机构相结合，具有监督、申诉和复核机制的综合考核评价体系。将各类交通运输违法行为列入失信记录。鼓励和支持各单位在采购交通运输服务、招标投标、人员招聘等方面优先选择信用考核等级高的交通运输企业和从业人员。对失信企业和从业人员，要加强监管和惩戒，逐步建立跨地区、跨行业信用奖惩联动机制。
  - 电子商务领域信用建设。
	  - 建立健全电子商务企业客户信用管理和交易信用评估制度，加强电子商务企业自身开发和销售信用产品的质量监督。推行电子商务主体身份标识制度，完善网店实名制。加强网店产品质量检查，严厉查处电子商务领域制假售假、传销活动、虚假广告、以次充好、服务违约等欺诈行为。打击内外勾结、伪造流量和商业信誉的行为，对失信主体建立行业限期禁入制度。促进电子商务信用信息与社会其他领域相关信息的交换和共享，推动电子商务与线下交易信用评价。完善电子商务信用服务保障制度，推动信用调查、信用评估、信用担保、信用保险、信用支付、商账管理等第三方信用服务和产品在电子商务中的推广应用。开展电子商务网站可信认证服务工作，推广应用网站可信标识，为电子商务用户识别假冒、钓鱼网站提供手段。
  - 统计领域信用建设。
	  - 开展企业诚信统计承诺活动，营造诚实报数光荣、失信造假可耻的良好风气。完善统计诚信评价标准体系。建立健全企业统计诚信评价制度和统计从业人员诚信档案。加强执法检查，严厉查处统计领域的弄虚作假行为，建立统计失信行为通报和公开曝光制度。加大对统计失信企业的联合惩戒力度。将统计失信企业名单档案及其违法违规信息纳入金融、工商等行业和部门信用信息系统，将统计信用记录与企业融资、政府补贴、工商注册登记等直接挂钩，切实强化对统计失信行为的惩戒和制约。
  - 中介服务业信用建设。
	  - 建立完善中介服务机构及其从业人员的信用记录和披露制度，并作为市场行政执法部门实施信用分类管理的重要依据。重点加强公证仲裁类、律师类、会计类、担保类、鉴证类、检验检测类、评估类、认证类、代理类、经纪类、职业介绍类、咨询类、交易类等机构信用分类管理，探索建立科学合理的评估指标体系、评估制度和工作机制。
  - 会展、广告领域信用建设。
	  - 推动展会主办机构诚信办展，践行诚信服务公约，建立信用档案和违法违规单位信息披露制度，推广信用服务和产品的应用。加强广告业诚信建设，建立健全广告业信用分类管理制度，打击各类虚假广告，突出广告制作、传播环节各参与者责任，完善广告活动主体失信惩戒机制和严重失信淘汰机制。
  - 企业诚信管理制度建设。
	  - 开展各行业企业诚信承诺活动，加大诚信企业示范宣传和典型失信案件曝光力度，引导企业增强社会责任感，在生产经营、财务管理和劳动用工管理等各环节中强化信用自律，改善商务信用生态环境。鼓励企业建立客户档案、开展客户诚信评价，将客户诚信交易记录纳入应收账款管理、信用销售授信额度计量，建立科学的企业信用管理流程，防范信用风险，提升企业综合竞争力。强化企业在发债、借款、担保等债权债务信用交易及生产经营活动中诚信履约。鼓励和支持有条件的企业设立信用管理师。鼓励企业建立内部职工诚信考核与评价制度。加强供水、供电、供热、燃气、电信、铁路、航空等关系人民群众日常生活行业企业的自身信用建设。

#### - 司法公信
- 司法公信是社会信用体系建设的重要内容，是树立司法权威的前提，是社会公平正义的底线。
- 法院公信建设。提升司法审判信息化水平，实现覆盖审判工作全过程的全国四级法院审判信息互联互通。推进强制执行案件信息公开，完善执行联动机制，提高生效法律文书执行率。发挥审判职能作用，鼓励诚信交易、倡导互信合作，制裁商业欺诈和恣意违约毁约等失信行为，引导诚实守信风尚。
- 检察公信建设。进一步深化检务公开，创新检务公开的手段和途径，广泛听取群众意见，保障人民群众对检察工作的知情权、参与权、表达权和监督权。继续推行“阳光办案”，严格管理制度，强化内外部监督，建立健全专项检查、同步监督、责任追究机制。充分发挥法律监督职能作用，加大查办和预防职务犯罪力度，促进诚信建设。完善行贿犯罪档案查询制度，规范和加强查询工作管理，建立健全行贿犯罪档案查询与应用的社会联动机制。

### **INTRODUCTION（修订）**

2016年起，苏州市设立"桂花分"系统，覆盖全市常住人口和流动人口约1300多万人。这套系统将打车爽约、网络游戏外挂作弊、订酒店不入住、预约餐厅不出现、实名手机欠费、叫外卖不取餐等25条日常行为纳入扣分项目，同时对志愿公益服务和献血进行加分（Suzhou Social Credit System 2016）。一个人的信用评分影响其贷款利率、子女升学、旅行出行，这意味着在苏州，每个人的每一个日常选择都被量化、监视、评判。

苏州并非孤例。从个人信用扩展到企业信用，中国正在全国范围内铺开信用管理的微观权力网络。这一转变始于2014年。国务院颁布《社会信用体系建设规划纲要（2014—2020年）》，这份纲要用14个主要章节详细规定了企业诚信制度的建设（State Council 2014），从市场主体信用记录档案到企业信用等级评价，从守信激励到失信联合惩戒，从金融信用到政务信用。国家试图将经济社会的每一个角落都纳入信用评分体系。

企业信用系统比个人信用更加复杂，也更加深刻地重塑了国家与市场的关系。从2014年的制度设计到2025年的实际运行，中国企业社会信用体系经历了从"统一惩罚"到"分散修复"的转变。2018-2019年间，失信企业黑名单数量爆炸式增长：2018年12月单月新增59,408户企业，到2019年6月这个数字飙升至217,067户，环比增幅209%（CODS 2019a; CODS 2019b）。这一扩张制造了合法性危机：大规模惩罚如何能够公正？

到2020年后，国务院改变了策略。它不是强化中央集中控制，而是推行"地方创新试点"，将修复权力分散到地方和行业部门（State Council 2020）。2022年，中央明确要求建立规范化的信用修复制度（Central and State Council 2022）。到2026年4月，《信用修复管理办法》正式实施。

**但这里隐藏着一个悖论**。官方政策宣称要建立"规范化、民主化"的修复制度，承诺程序明确、标准清晰、申诉渠道畅通。然而，对比惩罚和修复两套机制，我们发现了结构性的不对称：惩戒措施被精确制度化，修复权力却保持高度模糊。

2025年发布的《全国失信惩戒措施基础清单（2025年版）》对14类惩戒措施进行了详尽定义，具体规定了每一类惩戒的触发条件和适用范围。但同年颁布的《信用修复管理办法》对修复标准的界定却截然不同。办法第14条规定修复需要"纠正失信行为，完全履行失信行为涉及的行政处罚、异常名录或严重失信主体名单等规定的义务"，但"纠正"和"完全履行"在整份法律中都没有明确定义，这赋予了行政部门巨大的解释空间（State Administration for Market Regulation 2026）。

数据证实了这一权力结构的真实效果：2025年仅1.49万户企业被新增列入黑名单，而累计获得信用修复的企业达4579万户（State Administration for Market Regulation 2026）。这304倍的差异意味着：企业获得的不是修复的"权利", 即满足客观条件即自动恢复名誉. 而是修复的"许可"，即由行政机构根据模糊的标准决定是否批准企业的修复申请。

本文主张 中国企业社会信用系统的演变，代表了一种新的非制度化权力运作逻辑。通过将惩罚措施制度化（规范、明确、可预期）而将修复权力保持模糊（标准未定、评估灵活、权力分散），国家在看似规范的外壳下，强化了对企业信用地位的终极掌控。

### **背景：权力形态的三阶段演化（Background）**

2014年《社会信用体系建设规划纲要（2014—2020年）》的出台标志着中央对企业信用的系统性掌控开始（State Council 2014）。这份纲要用14个主要部分规定了企业诚信体系的构建方式：企业信用信息的记录、归集、共享、公开；守信激励和失信惩戒的联动机制；以及覆盖金融、工商、税务、质量、环保等多个领域的行业信用评价体系。其核心逻辑是"透明性约束"：通过统一的全国信用信息共享平台，将所有失信信息公开，使企业的失信行为无所遁形。

这一制度在实践中快速扩张。2018年9月，全国信用信息共享平台上线仅三年，就已累计归集黑名单信息约1364万条（Xinhuanet 2018）。黑名单的规模庞大，但制度看起来运行良好——惩罚有据可查，信息透明可得。

然而，从2018年底到2019年中期，执法的升级暴露了制度内部的矛盾。2018年12月，全国新增失信黑名单企业59,408户（CODS 2019a）；到2019年6月，单月新增企业达217,067户，环比增幅达209.30%（CODS 2019b）。黑名单数量的几何级增长制造了合法性困境：如果如此大规模的企业被列入黑名单，那么这些企业如何能够通过法律途径恢复声誉？大规模惩罚是否真的代表了制度的有效性，还是反映了某种失控的扩张？

2020年底，面对黑名单膨胀带来的合法性危机，国务院改变了策略（State Council 2020）。与其强化中央集中控制，反而选择了权力下移. 政府鼓励地方开展社会信用体系建设创新试点，推进"分级分类监管"。这一政策似乎传递出这样一种信号, 通过将权力分散到地方和行业部门，国家可以在看似"民主化"的名义下，扩大对失信标准定义和修复权力的自由裁量空间。

这一转向在2022年的政策文件中得到正式确认。中共中央办公厅和国务院办公厅联合发文《关于推进社会信用体系建设高质量发展促进形成新发展格局的意见》，明确要求建立健全信用修复制度，并强调各行业主管部门应当制定本领域的修复规范（Central and State Council 2022）。修复制度的出现，乍看之下是对失信企业的"救赎通道"，但实质上是权力形态的转换：从单向的惩罚扩张，转变为惩罚与修复的双轨制。国家既保持了惩罚的威慑力，又通过修复权力的模糊性保留了对企业信用地位的终极掌控权。

2024-2025年，修复制度开始法律化。《2024—2025年社会信用体系建设行动计划》明确了修复工作的规范化方向（NDRC 2024）。2025年11月19日，国家发改委委务会议审议通过《信用修复管理办法》，规定于2026年4月1日起施行（State Administration for Market Regulation 2025）。同年，国务院发布了《全国失信惩戒措施基础清单（2025年版）》，对14类惩戒措施进行了详尽的规范定义（State Council 2025）。

但这个"法律化"过程掩盖了一个结构性的不对称。惩戒措施被明确定义到具体条件和适用范围，而修复标准保持高度模糊。《修复办法》第14条列举修复的形式条件：达到最短公示期限、纠正失信行为、履行法定义务、作出信用承诺。然而"纠正失信行为"和"完全履行义务"的具体标准无处可查. 这留给了认定部门巨大的解释空间（State Administration for Market Regulation 2026）。更关键的是，第13条的"谁认定、谁修复"原则，将修复权力分散到各个行业主管部门和地方政府，这意味着同样的失信行为在不同地区、不同部门可能获得完全不同的修复结论。

2025年的统计数据证实了这一权力结构：仅1.49万户企业被新增列入黑名单，而累计获得信用修复的企业达4579万户（State Administration for Market Regulation 2026）。这304倍的差异所代表的是国家权力形态的重大转变。在这套制度下，企业不再拥有修复的"权利" whicih 即当其满足客观的法定条件时，可以自动地、无可争议地恢复信用地位。相反，企业只拥有修复的"许可", 其恢复取决于由行政机构根据模糊标准做出的个案判断。这一区别决定了国家对企业信用命运的根本掌控。

**Regional Implementation and Sectoral Differentiation (2014-2025)**

The 2014 framework mandated decentralized implementation. Regions and sectors developed  
diverse credit systems reflecting local conditions.

The Construction Contractor Credit Scoring System (CCCS) exemplifies sectoral targeting.  
Launched in Beijing before 2019, CCCS applied quantitative scoring to assess contractor  
compliance for project allocation. CCCS scores became decisive for obtaining large and  
mega-project contracts. Contractors with higher initial scores subsequently increased faster  
than low-scoring peers, demonstrating that credit scoring functioned primarily as reward for  
already-compliant firms rather than incentive for improvement.

The Credit Demonstration City Project (CDCC) represents territorial expansion. Launched  
jointly by the National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC) and People's Bank of  
China in 2015-2016, CDCC selected 41 pilot cities from 333 municipalities nationwide. Each  
city improved credit information disclosure through unified platforms and big data  
technology. CDCC enhanced entrepreneurial activity and alleviated credit constraints, but  
effects were significantly stronger in regions with weaker Confucian and Buddhist traditions.  
This differential impact reveals that uniform national standards generated inconsistent  
outcomes across heterogeneous territories.

**Toward Legal Formalization: Credit Repair as Administrative Response**

Regional fragmentation forced institutional adaptation. Because CCCS and CDCC  
demonstrated that uniform standards created arbitrary differentiation, post-2020 policy  
delegated remediation authority to regional and sectoral departments. Yet adaptation required  
administrative discretion. The scale of blacklisting—millions of enterprises—created urgent  
pressure to establish systematic remediation pathways.

By 2025, the state resolved this tension through legal formalization. The 2025 Basic List of  
Unified Punishment Measures precisely defined fourteen sanction categories. Simultaneously,  
the Credit Remediation Administrative Measures, effective April 2026, established formal  
remediation procedures. These mechanisms appeared to address the legitimacy crisis by  
creating transparent standards for both punishment and recovery. Yet this simultaneous  
institutionalization of punishment and remediation masked a fundamental asymmetry:  
punishment measures received precise definition while remediation standards remained  
deliberately ambiguous. Article 13's principle "whoever determines enforces remediation"  
dispersed authority across 31 provinces and multiple departments. Article 14 required  
"correcting violations" and "fully performing obligations" without defining these terms. The  
following section analyzes how this asymmetry transformed credit repair from a legal  
entitlement into an administrative permission, fundamentally preserving the discretionary  
state power that rapid expansion had exposed.

**Institutionalization of Credit Repair in China’s Social Credit System**

After 2020, China’s social credit system confronted a crucial dilemma: the mass inclusion of  
businesses on blacklists created a systematic need for credit repair as a tool to support  
economic activity. The state’s response was to establish a comprehensive credit repair  
mechanism, which by 2025-2026 had taken on a formally unified yet internally contradictory  
character.

In 2025, a key step in the development of credit repair was the adoption of the Administrative  
Measures for Credit Repair, Order No. 36 of the State Council for Development and Reform  
of the PRC approved on 19 November 2025 and came into force on 1 April 2026. This  
document establishes the first nationwide legal framework for the restoration of the credit  
standing of legal entities, entrepreneurs and other entities. According to this regulatory act,  
credit repair is defined as: an action whereby, following the rectification of breaches and the  
fulfilment of obligations, the competent authorities cease the public disclosure of negative  
credit information, cease its exchange and use, and lift the relevant sanctions in accordance  
with the law. This definition formally establishes that credit repair is not automatic removal  
of records but a process involving a change in the status of information about individuals in  
national and sector specific databases.

**Procedural Framework and Standardized Application Mechanisms**

In accordance with articles 13-16 of the Administrative Measures, applications must be  
submitted exclusively via the national credit information portal Credit China is the central  
platform for the publication and exchange of credit data. The programme has standardized  
eligibility criteria, under which an applicant may only submit a request under specific  
conditions, all of these conditions must be fulfilled. The minimum period for the publication  
of information regarding the violation (as defined by law or industry regulation) has elapsed,  
the violation has been rectified and all related administrative obligations have been fulfilled,  
the entity provides a public credit undertaking guaranteeing that the submitted materials are  
accurate and that it is prepared to bear responsibility in the event of false information -  
imperative that every of these requirements be strictly adhered to. Including that any other  
requirements established by law and regulations have been met.

The national platform must notify the applicant of the outcome within 10 working days of the  
submission. The authority that identified the beach must decide within 3 working days  
whether to accept or refuse the request. Once a decision to rectify the data has been made, the  
authority has a further 7 days to update the data (with a possible extension in complex  
circumstances).

**Differentiation of Violations and the Tiered Credit Repair Model**

Action Plan for Improving the Credit Repair System government documents establish a three  
tier classification system for dishonest information divided into minor, standard and serious.  
It is important because for minor violations (such as warnings or small fines) short

publication periods or even no publication at all apply. Rectifying such violations allows for a  
faster application for credit repair.

For serious violations (e.g. licence revocation or long term sanctions) information is  
published for, at least, 1 year and credit repair is only available after this period in accordance  
with established industry standards. This approach is a direct result of government guidelines  
aimed at creating a unified, standardized and differentiated credit restoration mechanism.

**Policy Objectives for Regulatory Unification and Limitation in Credit Repair Process**

The State Council explains that credit repair is intended not only to restore the right of bona  
fide enterprises to participate in the market but, also, to optimize the business environment,  
support economic activities and strengthen public confidence. Specific points of the Plan  
include: standardization of the credit information publication system on the Credit China  
platform; clarification of the channels for submitting applications for reinstatement;  
simplification of the documents that entities are required to submit; the obligation for sectoral  
authorities to clarify their standards and align them with national ones. These provisions  
underscore the drive to unify the legal framework, an issue that had been one of the main  
challenges up to 2025 due to regional differences and the lack of a uniform procedure.

Although the credit repair mechanism formally recognises that credit standing is a right of the  
entity, practice shows that this right still depends on structural conditions and administrative  
assessment. An entity does not receive a guaranteed automatic restoration upon fulfilment of  
the conditions - the decision is made in accordance with the rules and procedures established  
by sectoral and local authorities but indirectly via the Credit China platform. While there are  
standards for processing times, individual cases may be subject to discretionary assessment  
by sectoral regulators. This creates an element of discretion on the part for the authorities  
potentially leading to a degree of uncertainty in the application of rules despite formal  
standardization.

Shift in regulatory logic

**From Punitive Governance to Integrated Regulatory Control**

Since 2020, China’s social credit system has shifted from a model based primarily on  
punishment to a more comprehensive regulatory framework that integrates sanctions, repair  
and preventive management. This transformation does not signify a reduction in state control  
but rather its structural reorganisation through a combination of formalization and  
administrative flexibility.

Between 2014 and 2020, the system functioned as a negative incentive mechanism with the  
main means of enforcement being the inclusion of entities on blacklist. According to official  
data from the NDRC, by the end of 2019, the national system had recorded over 12 million  
entities acting in bad faith. Also, over 17 million people were restricted from purchasing  
airline tickets and 5.5 million from using high-speed trains. These restrictions were  
implemented through a mechanism of joint sanctions, coordinated by over 40 government

bodies including banks, courts and market regulators. In practice, this meant that a single  
entry on the blacklist automatically triggered a chain reaction of sanctions across various  
sectors.

**Structural Limits of Mass Sanctions and Systematic Inefficiencies**

However, this model has created a structural problem. Based on data from the Supreme Court  
of the People’s Republic of China (2019) the list of ‘defaulting debtors’ included over 14  
million individuals and companies, at the same time, proportion of court rulings actually  
enforced remained limited, despite the scale of sanctions. This indicates that mass  
punishment did not automatically lead to behavioural correction but, instead, created a  
build-up of ‘blocked’ economic entities that lost access to the market.

The response to these restrictions was the gradual introduction of a new approach as set out in  
State Council documents. The Guiding Opinions on Further Improving the Social Credit  
System (2020) explicitly states the need to improve the credit repair mechanism and ensure  
that entities can exit the status of dishonesty after rectifying violations. This signified a  
fundamental shift: sanctions were no longer viewed as a final state but as a part of a  
regulatory cycle. By 2025, this logic had been institutionalized through two main documents.  
First, the National Basic List of Disciplinary Measures for Dishonest Conduct (2025 edition)  
clearly limited the list of sanctions to 14 categories, prohibiting the arbitrary expansion of  
penalties. Second, Measures for Credit Repair (2025) formalize credit restoration procedures.  
Thus, the system shifted from expansive punishment to a regulated balance between  
sanctions and restoration.

**Legal Standardization and Restriction of Arbitrary Sanctions**

The new generation of regulation is aimed at curbing the arbitrary imposition of sanctions.  
For instance, the Basic List of Punishment Measures (2025) explicitly states that all sanctions  
must have a clear legal basis. It is prohibited to create ‘hidden lists of penalties’ outside the  
approved list. In 2022, the State Council emphasized that no additional sanctions may be  
imposed without a legislative basis. Formally, this represents a transition to rule based  
governance where the state limits its own punitive instruments.

Despite formal standardization, practical implementation reveals a different trend. The credit  
repair mechanism still remains decentralized, depends on interpretation of the specific  
authority and involves an assessment of rectification of the breach without clear criteria.  
Thus, the state limits its discretion in punishment (through the standardization of sanctions)  
but retains discretion in reinstatement (through the vagueness of criteria). This asymmetry  
creates a new regulatory logic where control is exercised not through punishment but by  
controlling access to the recovery of normal status.

**The regulatory paradox of Credit Recovery**

Problematique of the study lies not only in the internal evolution of the corporate component  
of China’s social credit system but also in the question of its comparative uniqueness within  
the global context of regulatory practices. At first glance, the institutionalization of the credit  
repair mechanism after 2020 may appear to be a move towards the rule based governance  
model standard in development economies where the sanctions are balanced by transparent  
procedures for restoring legal status. However, a deeper analysis reveals that the Chinese  
approach forms a qualitatively different logic of regulation. Not merely a system of  
accountability but a mechanism for selective access to economic normality.

The scale of sanctions in China has no direct equivalent in most other jurisdictions. In 2019,  
over 14.5 million entities were placed on defaulting debtors lists by Chinese courts and travel  
restrictions affected over `1 million people. Such statistics demonstrate not only a high level  
of enforcement but, also, a systemic dependence of economic participation on administrative  
status. By comparison, in EU countries or the US, credit sanctions (e.g. bankruptcy or credit  
ratings) are of considerably more limited nature and are not accompanied by such broad  
cross sectoral restrictions (e.g. travel bans or restrictions on access to public services).

This work looks at how corporate credit regulation in China’s Social Credit System (SCS) has  
expanded between 2024 and 2026. It focuses on how the system is changing from a  
fragmented set of rules into more organized, legally grounded, and enforcement-focused tool  
for managing corporate behavior. Contrary to common misconceptions the SCS is not a  
single scoring system. Instead, it is a decentralized framework that collects regulatory data  
across industries to guide companies and build trust in the market.

The study argues that 2024-2026 marks a major shift from experimental governance to a  
more structured system. The 2024-2025 Action Plan and the 2025 Guidelines show that  
Chinese authorities are working to make corporate credit regulation more standardized,  
interoperable, and enforceable. Rather than creating entirely new rules the system strengthens  
existing laws by combining administrative, judicial, and financial data into unified  
compliance profiles. This shows a broader strategy where regulation grows not through new  
legislation but through better coordination, visibility, and consequences for non compliance.

A key focus of the analysis is the system’s growing enforcement tools. Corporate regulation  
now uses blacklists, sector-specific ratings, and joint punishment systems that limit access to  
markets, subsidies, and financial tools for companies that break the rules. Expanding industry  
specific blacklists and stricter penalties show a move toward risk-based regulation, where a  
company’s reputation affects its economic opportunities. At the same time, the introduction  
of formal “credit repair” options (start in 2026) allows companies to correct past mistakes  
making the system fairer and encouraging compliance rather than permanent exclusion.

The analysis also argues that China corporate SCS represents a hybrid form of state  
capitalism based on data driven oversight. By centralizing and digitizing compliance data  
authorities create “regulatory visibility” where companies are monitored across areas like  
taxes, environmental rules, and contract performance. This shifts enforcement from reacting  
after violations to ongoing monitoring reducing information gaps between regulators and  
businesses.

However, the system is not just about punishment. It also uses positive incentives as red  
listing and administrative support to reward compliant firms. This dual approach (punishing  
rule-breakers while rewarding those who follow them ) shows that the SCS works more as  
reputation-based governance system than a simple surveillance tool, aligning company  
behavior with state-defined norms.

Finally, this illustrates these changes in the broader discussion of digital governance and  
regulatory capacity. Reforms in 2025, including better data protection and limits on misuse of  
credit information, show that authorities are aware of risks like overreach and data abuse.

This suggests that expanding the SCS goes hand-in- hand with efforts to make it more  
legitimate and stable through legal safeguards.

The tension stems from China’s historically exam‑driven school system where intense  
competition for limited university places fuels extensive homework, reliance on private  
tutoring, and elevated psychological and social pressures. The 2021 “double reduction”  
policy represented intervention to date in this domain, yet structural pressures persisted,  
revealing the limits of previous reforms. The 2026 policy package announced by the Ministry  
of Education marks more holistic attempt to re‑balance educational incentives and reduce  
academic pressure. These reforms go beyond limiting homework and regulatory oversight of  
private tutoring to embed “student well‑being” as an explicit policy objective. New measures  
include strict bans on excessive homework, limitations on high stakes examinations used to  
rank students, requirements for daily physical activity, and adequate break time.

A central analytical focus of this work is the policy logic underlying the 2026 package.  
Rather than viewing these measures as isolated adjustments this work interprets them as part  
of a sustained effort to restructure incentives in a highly competitive educational ecology. The  
reforms acknowledge that academic pressure is not merely function of homework volume or  
tutoring access, but also of structural incentives tied to examinations and teacher evaluations.  
By prohibiting schools from using test rankings for internal evaluations and delinking teacher  
performance from student exam scores policymakers are attempting to recalibrate the reward  
structures that have historically driven intense performance pressures.

However, this analysis assesses the implementation challenges of these reforms. Past policies  
like the double reduction initiative achieved mixed results. While they curtailed formal  
tutoring and reduced homework time they also inadvertently shifted learning pressures  
underground or exacerbated inequalities. Parents with resources found alternative ways to  
support children academic advancement. The 2026 reforms must be evaluated not only on  
their regulatory design but on how they interact with deeply embedded social expectations,  
labor market incentives, and local implementation capacities. This analysis explores whether  
policy signals from Beijing translate into substantive changes in classroom practices, family  
behaviors, and broader educational norms.

The analysis situates the 2026 policy package within a broader governance and sociopolitical  
context. It argues that these reforms reflect an emergent Chinese approach to educational

modernisation. One that tries to balance economic development imperatives with concerns  
about social stability, demographic trends, and the long‑term well‑being of younger  
generations. By reframing student well‑being as a policy priority and integrating health and  
development goals into educational standards, China’s 2026 reform agenda points toward an  
adaptive (if contested ) model.

In sum, this work contends that the 2026 education reform package represents an evolution in  
China’s efforts to reduce academic pressure. It offers a nuanced analytical framework for  
understanding both the transformative potential and the inherent constraints of these reforms  
in reshaping educational experiences and outcomes.
