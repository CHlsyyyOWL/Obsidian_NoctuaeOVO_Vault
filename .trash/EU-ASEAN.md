# Submit Proposal

Tags：Trade agreement, growth and development, New Structural Economics

**Theme Chosen**
1. EU & ASEAN
2. Growth and Unemployment

**Why this theme?**
The EU and ASEAN have been described as “natural trading partners.” Yet no region-to-region FTA currently exists. Instead, the EU has pursued bilateral FTAs with Singapore (2019) and Vietnam (2020), while talks with Indonesia, Thailand, and the Philippines are ongoing. These agreements are now seen as building blocks for a possible ASEAN–EU FTA. In the context of supply chain realignments, the Russia–Ukraine war, and intensifying US–China rivalry, the EU is seeking to re-establish its influence in Asia through economic partnerships. Through a new structure economic perspective, the comparative analysis of ASEAN member countries (mainly Singapore and Vietnam) would help improve the understanding of the resisting force to EU-ASEAN FTA, and the institutional spillovers from EU that would generate effects in ASEAN countries and stimulating growth under the NSE regime.

**Topic Area**
EU–ASEAN trade agreements, institutional spillovers, and structural transformation under New Structure Economics. 

The project will focus on Singapore and Vietnam,the two ASEAN members with comprehensive FTAs with the EU.as representative cases. By examining how EU rules on technical standards, labor, and sustainability are absorbed differently in a high-income hub (Singapore) and a middle-income manufacturing economy (Vietnam), the study explores how FTAs can serve as vehicles for industrial upgrading, investment attraction, and green transformation.

**Why this topic matters now**
The EU’s credibility as a global economic actor increasingly rests on its ability to project regulatory power in Asia, where FTAs can extend its “Brussels Effect” and re-shape regional standards. For ASEAN states, these agreements are not merely about market access but function as institutional packages that raise labor standards, improve SPS compliance, and promote green transition. From the perspective of New Structural Economics, they also help align factor endowments with higher-value industrial structures. The contrasting experiences of Singapore and Vietnam show how identical EU rules can produce divergent outcomes depending on institutional capacity and factor endowments, offering lessons for ASEAN’s diversity and highlighting why a region-to-region agreement remains difficult yet desirable. By identifying what works in these bilateral FTAs, the research aims to provide a roadmap toward an eventual ASEAN–EU FTA that is both development-friendly and geopolitically significant.
